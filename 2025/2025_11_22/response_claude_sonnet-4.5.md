## **Who Created It and When**

William Hogarth created "A Rake's Progress" as a series of eight paintings between 1732-1734, which he then engraved in 1734 and published in print form in 1735. This timing was strategic—Hogarth actually published the engravings on the very day that the Engravers' Copyright Act became law, protecting his work from piracy.

## **What's a "Rake"?**

The term "rake" in 18th-century England referred to a rakehell—a wealthy, immoral man who lived a life of vice, debauchery, and reckless spending. Think of it as the Georgian equivalent of a trust fund kid gone wild. The series follows Tom Rakewell, a spendthrift merchant's son who inherits money, wastes it all on luxurious living, prostitution and gambling, and ends up imprisoned and ultimately in Bethlem Hospital (Bedlam), London's infamous mental asylum.

## **Hogarth's Class Background and Perspective**

Hogarth came from a middle-class background—his father was a failed schoolteacher and writer. This is crucial to understanding the work. Hogarth wasn't painting from within the aristocracy, but as an outsider looking at both the upper classes and lower classes with a satirical, moralizing eye. He was essentially creating visual social commentary, criticizing the excesses of the wealthy while also showing the dangers of moral corruption.

## **Target Audience and Distribution**

Here's where it gets really interesting. The original paintings were for wealthy patrons, but Hogarth's genius was in the engravings. By publishing them as prints, he made the work accessible to a much broader middle-class audience. This was revolutionary—he was essentially creating mass-market art with moral lessons. The prints could be bought relatively cheaply and hung in homes, taverns, and shops. 

His target audience was the emerging middle class—people who had some education and money but weren't aristocrats. They could afford the prints and would appreciate both the moral warnings (don't squander your inheritance!) and the satirical jabs at upper-class vice.

## **Context and Social Commentary**

1730s London was experiencing rapid economic growth, and there was anxiety about "new money"—people who inherited or made fortunes but lacked the moral education to use wealth responsibly. The series shows specific London locations like the Rose Tavern brothel in Covent Garden, White's gambling club, St Marylebone Parish Church, Fleet Prison, and Bedlam—all real places his audience would recognize.

The work also critiques specific aspects of Georgian society:
- The exploitation of the wealthy by hangers-on and con artists
- The fashion for French culture (which Hogarth loathed, showing it in Scene 2)
- The brutal treatment of debtors and the mentally ill
- The callousness of the upper classes (wealthy women visiting Bedlam as entertainment!)

## **Effect and Legacy**

"A Rake's Progress" was enormously influential. It helped establish narrative art—telling stories across multiple images. Filmmaker Alan Parker described the works as an ancestor to the storyboard. The series inspired numerous adaptations, including Stravinsky's opera, ballets, films, and modern reinterpretations by artists like David Hockney and Grayson Perry.

The modern rake might fall through social media addiction, cryptocurrency scams, lifestyle inflation, and the gig economy rather than gambling dens and debtors' prisons—but the moral arc would be strikingly similar.

The brilliance of Hogarth's work is that these human weaknesses—vanity, greed, short-sightedness, and the exploitation of the foolish by the cunning—remain remarkably constant across three centuries.
