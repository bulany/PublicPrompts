## Learning Science Terms Explained

**SM-2 (SuperMemo 2)**: An algorithm from 1988 by Piotr Woźniak that schedules review of flashcards based on how well you remembered them. If you get something right easily, you see it again in 5 days. Get it wrong? Tomorrow. It's the foundation of modern spaced repetition.

**SRS (Spaced Repetition System)**: The general concept that reviewing material at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days...) is more efficient than cramming. Anki, the popular flashcard app, uses this.

**FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler)**: A newer, more sophisticated algorithm that improves on SM-2 by considering more variables and using machine learning.

**Leitner System**: A simpler physical method (1970s) using boxes. Cards you know well move to boxes reviewed less frequently; cards you struggle with stay in the "review daily" box.

**"Short Loop" / Immediate Correction**: When a learner makes an error, they get instant feedback and immediate opportunity to correct it. Research shows this is more effective than delayed feedback for motor skills and discriminative learning.

**Scaffolding**: Temporary support structures that help learners accomplish tasks they couldn't do alone, then gradually removed. Like training wheels on a bike.

**Discriminative Learning**: Learning to distinguish between similar stimuli and respond differently to each. Your ability to hear when a melody is "wrong" is discriminative learning - you've learned what the correct pattern sounds like, even if you can't produce it yet.

## Deliberate Practice: Key Principles

Deliberate practice involves structured activities specifically designed to improve current performance, requiring focused practice with immediate feedback allowing learners to self-adjust before the next task. The core idea: **quality over quantity**. 

Key components from Ericsson's work:
1. **Specific goals** targeting weaknesses
2. **Immediate feedback** for corrections
3. **Intense concentration** (not autopilot)
4. **Stepping outside comfort zone** repeatedly
5. **Mental representations** - experts develop sophisticated internal models

**Important note**: There's significant debate about deliberate practice, with recent research showing it explains only 14-26% of variance in expert performance. It's important but not the whole story.

## Optimal Chunk Size - The Research

Working memory can hold about 4 chunks in young adults (Cowan's finding), while Miller's famous "7 plus or minus 2" refers to items of typical complexity that compress to about 4 chunks. Research by Dirlam found that 3-4 items per chunk is mathematically optimal.

**For your game, I recommend:**

**Initial Learning Phases**: **5 departments at a time** (departments 1-5, 6-10, etc.)
- Within the 3-4 chunk sweet spot
- Small enough to feel achievable quickly
- Builds confidence fast

**Progressive Expansion**: Once mastered (80%+ accuracy), add the next 5
- 1-10 (two chunks of 5)
- 1-15 (three chunks of 5)
- Continue building

**Why NOT by region initially**: Regions vary wildly (Île-de-France has 8, Corse has 2, some have 13). Sequential numbering (1-5, 6-10) creates consistent cognitive load and leverages the **numerical sequence** you're already familiar with.
