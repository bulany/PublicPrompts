## 5. A response poem — *“If—” rewritten with moral consciousness*

Instead of “If—”, I’ll call this **“If You Must”** — a poem that keeps courage and discipline, but refuses moral blindness.

This is *not* Kipling’s voice.
It’s closer to **Frederick Douglass’s moral fire**, **James Baldwin’s vigilance**, and **Martin Luther King Jr.’s insistence that character without justice is hollow**.

---

### **If You Must**

If you must be strong,
be strong enough to question
what your strength is serving.

If you must endure,
endure not just the pain that sharpens you
but the truth that humbles you.

If you can keep your head
when others lose theirs,
ask first whether they are shouting in hatred
or crying because they are unheard.

If you can work without worshipping work,
strive without mistaking effort for virtue,
and rest without calling it failure,

If you can stand tall
without standing on another’s neck,
lead without demanding silence,
win without calling it destiny,

If you can speak of courage
without romanticizing violence,
of discipline
without mistaking it for obedience,

If you can fill your minutes
not just with motion
but with meaning,
and your hours
not just with striving
but with care,

If you can inherit the world
and ask who was dispossessed,
can hold power
and still feel accountable,

If you can look into history
without flinching,
and into yourself
without lying,

Then you may grow into something rarer than a man:
a human being
whose strength does not require domination,
whose success does not depend on forgetting,
whose legacy is not conquest
but repair.

### 6. Was Kipling a "Broken Man"?

The evidence suggests yes.

* **Physical Decline:** After 1915, he suffered from severe, undiagnosed gastric ulcers (likely stress-induced) that caused him constant pain until his death.
* **The Change in Writing:** His pre-war writing is bouncy, rhythmic, and confident ("Jungle Book", "If—"). His post-war writing becomes dark, complex, fragmented, and obsessed with pain and healing.
* **The Epitaph:** Perhaps the most heartbreaking evidence is a short couplet he wrote later in the war, widely interpreted as his own confession regarding his son:
> *"If any question why we died,*
> *Tell them, because our fathers lied."*
