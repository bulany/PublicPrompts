# French Road Classification System

French roads follow a logical hierarchical system where the **letters** indicate the road class and the **numbers** often (but not always) have geographic or historical meaning.

**A (Autoroutes)** - Motorways/Freeways
- Highest-capacity roads, typically 2+ lanes each direction
- Usually tolled (p√©age), though some free sections exist
- Speed limit: 130 km/h (110 km/h in rain)

**N (Routes Nationales)** - National Roads
- Major roads maintained by the national government
- Often former autoroutes or major historic routes
- Speed limit: 80-110 km/h depending on configuration
- Red signs with white text

**D (Routes D√©partementales)** - Departmental Roads

**M (Routes M√©tropolitaines)** - Metropolitan Roads

## The Numbering Logic

### Autoroutes (A-roads)

The A-road numbers follow a **radial pattern from Paris**:

**A1-A9**: Major routes radiating from Paris like spokes on a wheel
- **A1**: Paris ‚Üí Lille (north)
- **A4**: Paris ‚Üí Strasbourg (east)
- **A6**: Paris ‚Üí Lyon (southeast) - one of the most important!

**A10-A99**: Secondary routes, cross-country connections, and regional autoroutes

**A100+**: Ring roads and metropolitan bypasses
- **A86**: Outer Paris ring road

### Routes Nationales (N-roads)

Similar radial logic from Paris, though many N-roads have been downgraded to D-roads:
- **N1-N20**: Historically radiated from Paris
- The famous **N7** (Paris ‚Üí Mediterranean) was celebrated in French culture

## Historical Context

France's road system reflects **centuries of Paris-centric governance**. The radial pattern dates to Roman times and was reinforced by:
- **17th-18th centuries**: Royal roads radiating from Versailles/Paris
- **1960s-1980s**: Modern autoroute construction following these historic axes
- **2006 reform**: Many N-roads transferred to departments, becoming D-roads (which is why you see high D-numbers)

The "missing" A2, A3, A5, A8, A9 aren't missing - they exist! But A6 and A10 are the real workhorses because they connect Paris to France's second city (Lyon) and the vacation destinations (Mediterranean, Atlantic).
