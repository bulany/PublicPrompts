## Frances Hodgson Burnett: Who She Was

First, just take a moment to register how extraordinary this woman's life story is — because it directly shaped everything in that book you're listening to.

Frances Eliza Hodgson Burnett was born on 24 November 1849 in Cheetham, Manchester, England, the daughter of Edwin Hodgson, an ironmonger from Doncaster in Yorkshire, and his wife Eliza. That Yorkshire connection matters — it's in her blood, literally. After her father died in 1853, when Frances was four years old, the family fell on strained circumstances, and in 1865 they emigrated to the United States, settling in New Market, Tennessee.

So imagine: a bookish, story-obsessed Manchester girl, suddenly transplanted to rural Tennessee, in a log cabin, with almost nothing. There the Hodgson family found itself in an unexpected place, and but for the generosity of their neighbours, they might have starved. And yet — Frances described these as her "Dryad days." For a city girl, the abundance of forests and mountains was overwhelming. Even in poverty, nature was already working its healing on her.

**She started writing purely to survive.** Frances began her writing career at age 19 to help earn money for the family, publishing stories in magazines. She once claimed that she never submitted a manuscript that wasn't published — an astonishing record born of sheer necessity.

## A Timeline

**1849** — Born, Manchester, England
**1853** — Father dies. Family falls into poverty
**1865** — Family emigrates to Tennessee
**1868** — Sells her first story to *Godey's Lady's Book*
**1873** — Marries Swan Burnett, a doctor, in Knoxville
**1874** — Son Lionel born
**1876** — Son Vivian born (the two boys would inspire Colin and Little Lord Fauntleroy respectively)
**1877** — First novel, *That Lass o' Lowrie's*, published to strong reviews — a story of Lancashire coal miners, drawing on her Manchester roots
**1886** — *Little Lord Fauntleroy* published. It was the Harry Potter of its day — a household name from the moment it was serialised, translated into over a dozen languages, sold over half a million copies. It made her genuinely, spectacularly famous and wealthy
**1890** — Her sixteen-year-old son Lionel died of tuberculosis in her arms in Paris, and she had his casket covered in violets. This is the wound at the heart of everything that followed
**1890s** — Seeks solace in spiritualism, Theosophy, Christian Science, New Thought. She was intrigued by the idea of séances and communication with the departed, which was consolation to her after her son's death.
**1898** — Rents Great Maytham Hall in Kent. Divorces Swan Burnett
**1900** — Marries Stephen Townsend, an actor ten years her junior — her biographer Gretchen Gerzina writes that it was "the biggest mistake of her life."
**1902** — Divorces Townsend
**1898–1907** — Lives at Great Maytham Hall and writes in the walled rose garden. *The Secret Garden* takes shape here
**1905** — *A Little Princess* published. Becomes an American citizen
**1910–1911** — *The Secret Garden* serialised, then published in book form
**1924** — Dies peacefully at her Long Island home, aged 74. Her last words, almost, were: "As long as one has a garden one has a future, and as long as one has a future one is alive."

## Was Writing *The Secret Garden* a Breeze? Or Did She Struggle?

I wish I could tell you it was pure joy — and in some ways it was — but the truth is richer and sadder than that.

The book was written in the long aftermath of enormous suffering. After Lionel's death she wandered Europe in a daze, writing to her husband: "I am ill and more than unhappy... I would rather it got worse than better. I should like to die just as Boy did." That is a mother in the most raw, devastating grief. She genuinely wanted to die.

The Secret Garden, written nearly two decades later, is in many ways her answer to that grief — a meditation on whether the dead can be released, whether the living can be healed, whether something locked up and given up for dead can bloom again. Despite illness, grief, and exhaustion, she went on to write her most beloved tale. And here is the beautiful irony: Lionel is thought to have inspired Colin in the book — the sickly, bedridden boy convinced he is dying — and one can only wish that he could have had a similar transformation as Colin. She couldn't save her son in life, so she saved him on the page. Colin gets to live, get up, run, and discover the world. It is an act of profound, complicated love.

The physical writing of it, though, was apparently genuinely pleasurable. She would take her table and chair, her tuffet and rug, and a large Japanese umbrella into the walled garden at Maytham Hall and write there, surrounded by the roses she had planted herself. At the end of her life, she said one of her happiest memories was "of a softly raining spring in Kent when I spent nearly three weeks kneeling on a small rubber mat on the grass edge of a heavenly old herbaceous border bed... tucking softly into the rich sweet damp mould the plants which were to bloom in loveliness for me in the summer." That is Dickon's magic — she wasn't just describing it, she was living it.

## Why Don't You Know Her Name? Why Only Now?

This is a real injustice worth examining. Few people realise that The Secret Garden was only one of 53 novels she wrote and published, and that most of her books were for adults, not children. She would be amazed to know that this is the one for which she is most remembered today.

During her lifetime, *Little Lord Fauntleroy* was vastly more famous — it was a cultural phenomenon that shaped fashion, children's clothing, and popular culture on both sides of the Atlantic. The Secret Garden was beloved but not considered her masterpiece by her contemporaries.

Then there's the matter of how literary history has treated women. She was dismissed by serious critics as "sentimental," writing for children and mothers — genres that were chronically undervalued. By the early 1900s, Burnett's work had fallen out of favour, as the florid writing style of the 19th century was no longer in vogue. She was also, frankly, too cheerful, too interested in healing and magic and gardens — qualities that a cynical 20th-century literary establishment found lightweight. It took the second half of the 20th century for readers to rediscover that what looked like sentimentality was actually something far more complex — a genuine philosophical engagement with grief, nature, and what it means to be alive.

You're finding her now because *The Secret Garden* has never gone out of print, has kept finding new readers who sense something in it that matters, and because we are perhaps — collectively — in a moment when its themes speak particularly loudly.

## The Real Secret Garden

In the early 1900s, Frances Hodgson Burnett discovered an iron gate covered in ivy at Great Maytham Hall, and beyond it she saw glimpses of an overgrown walled garden. Peeking through the bars at the neglected blooms and tangled vines, a story began to form. The garden at Maytham dated from 1721 and had been entirely neglected. By her own account, aided by a robin, Burnett discovered the door hidden amongst the ivy, and began the restoration of the garden, which she planted with hundreds of roses.

She wrote about her real robin later: "I did not own the robin — he owned me — or perhaps we owned each other. He was an English robin and he was a person — not a mere bird. This particular one I knew in my rose-garden in Kent."

This detail is important for understanding the whole book: she wasn't inventing the robin who leads Mary to the key and the garden. She was transcribing something that actually happened to her.

## Dickon — Could He Have Actually Existed?

Yes. Absolutely yes. And this is perhaps the most fascinating part of your question.

Dickon is, in some sense, the spirit of Missel Moor. His eyes are described as looking like "pieces of moorland sky," and he smells of heather and grass and leaves — as if he were made of them. When the reader first encounters him, he is sitting beneath a tree charming animals with the music of his wooden pipe. This immediately, as scholars have noted, conjures the image of Pan — the Greek god of wild things. Throughout the novel, Mary compares him to an angel, and his understanding of the natural world makes him an almost mystical figure.

But here's the important thing: a boy like Dickon was not a fantasy in Victorian rural England — he was a type. A recognisable, documented type.

Wild squirrels were frequently kept as pets in the Victorian era, and people would catch them as babies to tame them. Ravens, an intelligent species, were also kept as pets — typically taken from the nest as fledglings, trained to roam the garden freely but return home. These aren't invented details of Dickon's world — they are straightforwardly accurate descriptions of what country children in Yorkshire did. Children who grew up working on the land, who spent all day outdoors in all weathers, who understood the seasons and the animals from infancy, could absolutely develop the kind of deep, intuitive rapport with wild creatures that Dickon has. The moor children of the North Riding had a reputation for this kind of uncanny naturalism.

In the novel, the robin's own narration confirms that Dickon speaks the robin language, thereby affirming his intimate relationship with the natural world. His animal companions include Captain the fox, Soot the crow, and Nut and Shell the two squirrels. All of these are animals that, in the Victorian countryside, were actually hand-reared by children from orphaned or injured young. A fox kit, a crow fledgling, a baby squirrel — anyone who raised one from infancy would have an animal that followed them around and sat in their pockets. It was not magic. It was patience, gentleness, and time.

What Burnett did was take this real rural type — the working-class country boy who belongs so completely to the natural world that he seems half-animal himself — and invest him with something more: a spiritual quality. In the second-to-last chapter, when Colin feels particularly full of Magic in the secret garden, Dickon leads the three of them in the Doxology — a song of praise to God. Hearing Dickon sing makes even Ben Weatherstaff admit, "I never seed no sense in th' Doxology afore, but I may change my mind i' time." Dickon is effectively a nature priest. His connection to country is itself a form of worship.

Whether Frances knew a specific Dickon — there's no documented "original" the way Vivian was the original for Fauntleroy — but she knew the people of rural Kent during her years at Maytham Hall. She socialised in the local villages and enjoyed the country life. She would have encountered the farm labourers' children, the boys who spent every waking hour on the land. She loved them and observed them carefully.

## Your Reading — Mary's Transformation

Your instinct about Mary's arc is exactly right, and it's the deepest thing in the book. She arrives in Yorkshire as the most alienated possible creature — shaped by colonial privilege in India, cut off from any real contact with place, dismissive of the "native" in all its forms. And what heals her is becoming, herself, a kind of native — of the moor, of the garden, of the cold Yorkshire air. She even begins to speak the dialect. Her transformation is not really about the garden in the ornamental sense; it's about *belonging to a place*, which is something she has never experienced.

The parallel you're drawing with Indigenous Australian ideas of "country" — that reciprocal, relational, deeply embodied relationship with specific land — is genuinely illuminating, not fanciful. The novel presents Dickon as both the guide and the embodiment of this relationship: Mary even compares his pipe-playing to how natives charm snakes in India, and she and Colin perceive him as thrillingly strange and exotic — like the Indian servants, he speaks a different language, in his case the Yorkshire dialect. Burnett is, perhaps unconsciously, inverting the colonial lens entirely: the "civilised" colonial child is the sick one, the damaged one, the one who needs to be healed, and the one who is most whole — most alive — is the boy who has the deepest relationship with country. That is a quietly radical inversion to put at the heart of a children's book in 1911.

As for how you might apply it to healing colonial wounds — that is a profound and difficult question that deserves much more space than I have here. But the essence of the book's answer seems to be: you cannot think your way back into relationship with country. You have to *do* it. You have to kneel in the soil. You have to notice the robin. You have to learn the dialect. You have to let the cold air get into your lungs. Mary doesn't become healed by reading about the moor — she heals by being *on* it. That embodied, patient, daily practice of attention and care is, I suspect, where the book's wisdom and indigenous relational knowledge most deeply meet.
