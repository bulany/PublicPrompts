# An ode to the Brodburger

Driving past late on cold Canberra nights I’d seen those caravan fairy lights. In their faint glow between the trees they half-illuminated a mystery. What could possibly be keep all those people standing out there in the freezing cold by this forgotten corner of Lake Burley Griffin?

I reasoned it must be some illegal gambling setup or a bizarre old-timers night-sailing club. Driving past another night with a friend I got more of the story: “Apparently, he’s a world famous chef, but now he only does burgers. Does ’em with blue cheese and brie and stuff – they’re meant to be really good. That’s what keeps the people standing out there.”

Curious to find out more, last Friday night I finally got my chance. Accessing the place is a challenge in itself – you have to approach from the right direction, but beware: once you find that turn-off, your life may change forever. I parked the car amongst the healthy crowd of others, still shaking my head as to how this place, miles from any movie theatres, shops or heating could draw a crowd at this hour. Getting out and edging my way into the huddle standing around the caravan I could tell I was impinging on a new culture with already established customs.

“Are you guys in line?” I gingerly asked the motley assembly of mostly blokes who looked as if they’d done this all before

“Line? Nah mate, there’s none of that… you’ve just gotta use the force or something. You’ll get the hang of it.”

There was something bizarre about this power dynamic. A group of half freezing guys staring into a cosy looking gypsy cabin, waiting on salvation.

I squinted to make out the menu. “Listen,” a beanied regular advised me, “if it’s your first time you’ll probably want to start with a blue cheese Brodburger, then you can work you way through the other options from there”.

Obviously he thought I’d be back to brave the cold again. “Look mate, it’s not uncommon for first-timers to score a Brodtrick off the bat”. “A Brod-trick?” I stammered. “Yeah”, he chuckled to his mate, “three nights in a row!”

It’s not only blokes who seem to be mad about these burgers. Another shiverer confided: “Every time I drive past here with my wife, she laments that we’ve just eaten, or that it’s not dinner-time. Now I have to bring the burgers back to her while she waits where it’s warm”.

The place was certainly getting a good rap from customers. Surveying the crowd of a dozen or so, you could see that underneath their veneer of blokey stoicness, these guys seemed almost proud of something. Jumping out of the caravan to grab more bread rolls out of his car, I took my chance to encounter the man behind operation that so fascinated me.

“I guess you must be Brod”, I tried unconvincingly.

His warm response seemed to wash away any nervousness I had. “Yeah, I am. Sasha actually. Sasha Brodbeck”.

Sporting only a black T-shirt against the cold, his relaxed and friendly manner seemed to draw me further into the enchantment of the place. I had time to learn that he’d had the place open just 4 months before he quickly jumped back behind the grill to keep up with the run of orders.

It was then however that the magic of that gypsy caravan really kicked into overdrive. “Right, is anyone waiting to order?” called the girl from inside, flicking a curl of black hair out of her equally dark eyes. It was my turn.

Her manner, her smile, the way she threw off the cajoling of the onlooking guys… everything was perfect. I wanted to film the Australian version of American Graffiti right there and then. There were no cash registers, no eftpos or state of the art ordering systems, or slick café fit-outs. It’s was just good old fashioned “show me the money and I’ll show you the burger”.

I briefly wondered if it was all a carefully engineered marketing scheme – did she hand me the free chips just to be nice, or could she tell I was fresh meat, the only customer she hadn’t seen before. Maybe there is a dark secret.

I can just imagine a black t-shirt clad Sasha making his pact with the devil, securing his place in the hearts of Canberran’s over the souls of a few hapless bovine beasts. Or maybe it’s just that there is some kind of gypsy caravan magic powering those fairy lights. In any case, it didn’t matter now, I was firmly under the Brodburger spell.

Enchanted.

My number is called and she hands over my burgers. I seem to be the last customer around, but someone who’s just a bit too far from the fairylight glow pipes up that he’s still waiting.

A contented but shivering burger-eater jokes “If you think they’re too slow, McDonalds is just down the road!” Everyone laughs, but behind the joke seems to lurk a threat: “Just you go and try it, punk”.

Somehow the fairylights and the fabled burgers have formed a faithful brotherhood. Going anywhere else is the last thing anyone has on their mind. It’s as if the wait in the cold is a test of the burger-eater’s mettle.

Sasha takes advantage of the lull in business to get out from behind the grill and light a cigarette. It’s 8pm and the night still feels young, although quite bitterly cold. As I walk away, burger in hand, I think it might be a lonely stretch for him from here till the end of the night. But before the thought has time to finish, a gang of six sports bikes roars in. Parking right up near the caravan, they are cursing and joking as they take off their helmets.

Where they have been sounds exciting – I’m almost tempted to lurk in the caravan light to glean some details of their exploits. Sasha stamps his cigarette out and jumps back behind the grill. Obviously they knew about this spot. Maybe they fell under caravan’s spell just like me.

Whether he has engineered it or not – Sasha Brodburg has created a real place. A place that will put Canberra on the map much more than the latest architectural masterpiece or grand monument. He, along with Bibimbap’s at Korean student joints and friendly staff at the Dendy cinemas are slowly giving Canberra what it’s been lacking for so long: the special sauce that Burley Griffin, no matter how meticulous he was, couldn’t fit into his plan.

Regardless of how good the burgers are (and you’ll have to judge them for yourselves), Brodburg should already be unanimously celebrated for that.

Driving away with my hard-won burger by my side, my thoughts run-riot about what summer may hold for Sasha and his caravan.

For him and for Canberra, I hope the gypsy magic of those fairy lights lasts a little longer.

August 2009

Here are my thoughts on your request, an analysis of the style you provided, and the draft of the article.

### Part 1: Analysis of "An Ode to the Brodburger"

To replicate the style effectively, we need to understand what makes the "Brodburger" piece work. It is not just a food review; it is a **myth-making narrative**.

**Key Stylistic Elements:**

1.  **The "Outsider" Narrative Arc:** The writer starts as a skeptic/observer on the periphery ("Driving past late," "illegal gambling"). They then cross a threshold ("approached from the right direction") and become an initiate. This mimics the Hero’s Journey on a micro-scale.
2.  **Atmospheric Contrast:** The piece relies heavily on the contrast between the *cold/hostile environment* (freezing Canberra night, forgotten corner, shivering crowd) and the *warmth/magic of the destination* (fairy lights, gypsy cabin, glowing interior).
3.  **Conversational Dialogue:** The quotes aren't rigid interviews; they are colloquial snippets ("Nah mate," "use the force") that establish a "tribe" mentality. It shows, rather than tells, that there is a community here.
4.  **Romanticizing the Mundane:** It elevates a burger van to a "gypsy caravan" and a cook to a "world-famous chef." It uses words like "enchantment," "spell," and "fabled."
5.  **The Philosophical Outro:** It ends by zooming out. It stops talking about the burger and starts talking about the *soul of the city*. It argues that "messy" human magic is better than "meticulous" city planning.

**Verdict:** It works because it sells a *feeling* rather than a product. It invites the reader into a secret club.

### Part 2: The "Tweetable" Suffix

You asked for examples similar to how we use **"-gate"** (Watergate) to imply a scandal. Here are a few "shorthand" suffixes or phrases used to signal a specific type of cultural event:

*   **"-gate" (Scandal/Coverup):** Used for *Pizzagate*, *Deflategate*, *Gamergate*. It implies a conspiracy or a leaked truth.
*   **"-calypse" or "-geddon" (Catastrophic change):** Used for *Retail-pocalypse*, *Carmageddon*, *Snowmageddon*.
*   **"The [X] Moment" (A paradigm shift):** Usually refers to *The iPhone Moment* (when tech becomes accessible) or *The Sputnik Moment* (a realization that you are behind).
*   **"Uber for [X]" (Disruption):** Used to describe the "gig-ification" of a service.

For your article, since we are talking about the democratization of media and the fall of barriers, **"The Gutenberg Moment"** or **"The Great Unmoating"** fits best.

# The Great Unmoating

I’ve been thinking a lot about the Beatles lately. specifically, that grainy black and white footage of the Shea Stadium concert. You know the one—thousands of teenagers losing their minds, screaming so loud the band couldn't even hear themselves play.

For years, I bought into the mythology: that this was pure, unadulterated magic. Lightning in a bottle. But looking at it now, through the lens of a world that is rapidly shifting beneath our feet, I see something else. I see a moat.

It was the "Studio Moat."

The Beatles were four talented guys, sure. But they were also four guys standing on the right side of a massive, expensive drawbridge. Back then, recording a high-fidelity album required resources akin to the Apollo program. You needed a cathedral of sound engineering, miles of magnetic tape, and an army of technicians. The separation between the *creator* and the *consumer* was absolute because the tools of creation were locked behind a paywall of impossible proportions.

But just like the Apollo guidance computer is now dwarfed by the smartphone in your pocket, the Studio Moat has been filled in.

I was reading that famous leaked memo from Google recently—the one where a researcher admitted, "We have no moat." They realized that their proprietary AI magic was leaking out, becoming accessible to anyone with a laptop. It made me realize: it’s not just Google. It’s all of us. We are all knowledge workers sitting in a film processing lab, unaware that the digital camera has just been invented.

Think of the medieval monks. For centuries, they sat in cold cells, hand-copying Bibles. It was holy work. It was skilled work. It was their "moat." Then Gutenberg arrived with his press. In an instant, the value of hand-copying plummeted. Was it a tragedy for the monks? Perhaps. But for the rest of human history, it was the spark of the Renaissance.

We are living through the Gutenberg Moment of creativity.

There’s a scene I recount often—perhaps apocryphal, but spiritually true—about the very first cinema screening in Lyon by the Lumière brothers. They showed a simple clip of a train arriving at a station. The audience, having never seen a moving image, reportedly screamed and ran to the back of the room, terrified the train would crush them.

We look back and laugh at their naivety, yet here we are, looking at Large Language Models and AI video generators, and we are running for the back of the room. We fear the train. We fear that our value as writers, coders, and artists is about to be run over.

And maybe it is.

I’ve always had a complicated relationship with John Lennon. "Imagine" is a beautiful anthem, a secular hymn for a utopia. "Imagine no possessions," he sang. It’s a lovely thought. But if I were to stand on a street corner and busk that song today, I’d be moved along by police or sued for copyright infringement. Lennon sang of a world without barriers from the comfort of a mansion built by royalties—built by the "Intellectual Property Moat."

The irony is thick. He asked us to imagine a world without greed, while his estate relied on the very violence of law to maintain his exclusivity. He could demand his gardener grow only white roses (as the stories go) because he held the keys to the kingdom.

But what happens when the keys are copied?

We are approaching a horizon where the "Avatar Moat" dissolves. Today, you need James Cameron and half a billion dollars to make a blockbuster. Tomorrow? You might just need a brilliant prompt and a video model.

Is this scary? Absolutely. Ask the typesetters at the New York Times or the chemists at the photo development labs. Their worlds ended. But did *the* world end? No. The writers kept writing. The photographers took more photos than ever before. WordPress—created by a modern-day Gutenberg, Matt Mullenweg—allowed millions of people to become publishers without ever touching a printing press.

We are losing our moats. The violence of exclusivity is fading. The "experts" are losing their monopoly.

But in exchange, we are gaining a world where the only barrier to entry is imagination. We are moving from a world of screaming fans worshipping four guys on a distant stage, to a world where everyone has a microphone.

It will be messy. It will be chaotic. But as the moats fill with earth and we can finally walk across them, I have to believe the view from the other side will be worth it.

Imagine that.
