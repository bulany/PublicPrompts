## Olomouc and Moravia — A Beginner's Guide

**The city itself**

Olomouc is a major city in Moravia and the capital of the administrative region of the same name. It is the sixth largest city in the Czech Republic and the most important city in the Haná region. Don't be fooled by the size: if Brno is Moravia's political and economic heart, Olomouc is the historic soul of the region. Rick Steves famously describes it as the country's second city in spirit.

The history of Olomouc dates back to at least the 7th century, when the countryside around Olomouc Hill became home to the first Slavic settlers. At the beginning of the 9th century, the local Slavic fortified settlement became one of the border strongholds of Great Moravia.

A bishopric was established there in 1063 and was raised to an archbishopric in 1777. Olomouc was considered the Moravian capital during the Thirty Years' War (1618–48), when it was occupied and plundered by the Swedes. The badly damaged town was displaced after 1640 by Brno as the foremost city of Moravia.

The city is also a major university town. Palacký University, the oldest in Moravia and second oldest in the Czech Republic, was founded in 1573. With over 25,000 students, it gives the city a youthful, energetic feel despite its deeply historical character.

The most famous landmark is the Holy Trinity Column: a Baroque religious monument completed in 1754 after 38 years of work. It is 35 metres tall and was built to express gratitude for surviving a plague outbreak in the early 18th century. All artists and craftsmen involved were from Olomouc — it was and still is a source of civic pride. It was inscribed on UNESCO's World Heritage List in 2000.

## Bohemia vs. Moravia — What's the Difference?

Bohemia is the westernmost and largest historical region of the Czech Republic. Moravia lies to its east. Together with Czech Silesia, they form what are historically called the Bohemian Lands — roughly the area of the modern Czech Republic.

The key thing to understand is that for much of Czech history, "Bohemian" and "Czech" were nearly synonymous. In the national language, "Czech" and "Bohemian" mean pretty much the same thing. Prague, which is in Bohemia, became the national capital — which some Moravians still grumble about today.

For nearly a millennium, up until the 20th century, Moravia governed itself with relative autonomy as one of the three historic "Lands" of the Bohemian Crown, along with Bohemia and Silesia.

As for cultural differences today: Moravians enjoy safeguarding regional identities — with their own dialects, traditions, and in more rural places celebrations, folk costumes, and music. Inhabitants of the Czech Republic's eastern half were typically characterised as "kind," "welcoming," and "friendly," but also "more patriotic and religious." Moravians also drink more wine (South Moravia has famous vineyards) and are proud of their fruit spirits, especially *slivovice* (plum brandy).

It's a gentle, affectionate rivalry rather than a real ethnic divide. Just as Bavarians or Saxons also consider themselves Germans, Moravians appear to increasingly consider themselves both Czech and Moravian at the same time — one is political, the other cultural.

## "Bohemian" the Lifestyle Word — Yes, It Connects to Czech Bohemia!

This is a delightful etymological story. The French word *bohémien* emerged in 15th-century France to refer to the Roma people, based on the false belief that they had come from Bohemia many years before.

The Romani originate from the northwestern Indian subcontinent and emigrated to Europe, arriving in France during the late Middle Ages. As a nomadic people with different social norms from Europeans, they were stereotyped in various ways. Because they arrived in France apparently *via* Bohemia (or were confused with Hussite religious refugees from Bohemia), the French called them *bohémiens*.

Then, in 19th-century Paris, French artists living primarily in the Latin Quarter lived in poverty — frequently by choice — to pursue their creative passions. These denizens were given the name "bohemians" because their low-rent lodgings were in areas with high Romani populations, and their itinerant, impoverished, romanticized artist lifestyles reflected, to wealthier Parisians, the Romani way of life.

So the chain goes: Bohemia (Czech region) → French *bohémien* (Roma people) → Parisian artists → the modern concept of a "bohemian" lifestyle.

## *La Bohème* — Yes, Directly Related

Absolutely. The transferred sense of "bohemian" in reference to unconventional living was popularised by Henri Murger's stories from the late 1840s, later collected as *Scènes de la Vie de Bohème* — the basis of Puccini's *La Bohème*. The Charles Aznavour song *La Bohème* that you're thinking of is also drawing on exactly this tradition — it describes the bohemian artist's life in Montmartre, which is the same cultural mythology. So yes: French song → Parisian artistic lifestyle → Romani associations → the name of a Czech region. It's all connected.

## Is Czech a Slavic Language? Is It Close to Polish or Slovak?

Yes, Czech is unambiguously a Slavic language. Czech is a member of the West Slavic sub-branch of the Slavic branch of the Indo-European language family. This branch includes Polish, Kashubian, Upper and Lower Sorbian and Slovak. Slovak is the most closely related language to Czech, followed by Polish.

To put it simply, Czech and Slovak are mutually intelligible — Czechs and Slovaks can largely understand each other without learning the other's language. Czech and Polish are related but *not* mutually intelligible: linguists claim Czech's oral intelligibility with Polish is only around 36%. Germans and Danish are in an entirely different branch of Indo-European (Germanic) and are not closely related to Czech at all — though German has historically influenced Czech vocabulary quite a lot due to centuries of Habsburg rule and proximity.

Think of it like the Romance languages: Czech, Slovak, and Polish are a bit like Spanish, Italian, and French — clearly related, sharing much grammar and vocabulary, but not instantly comprehensible to each other.

## The "Slave/Slav" Question — A Genuinely Complex and Sensitive Topic

This is one of the more fascinating and delicate areas of linguistics, and you've asked exactly the right follow-up questions.

**Does "slave" come from "Slav"?**

Most specialists agree that it does, though the debate continues. The connection is historical: from around the 8th–10th centuries, Slavic peoples in Central and Eastern Europe were frequently captured and sold into slavery across the Mediterranean and Islamic worlds. The Latin word *sclavus* (meaning "slave") appears to have derived from the Byzantine Greek name for Slavic peoples, *Sklabinoi*, because these people were so often the ones being enslaved at that time and place in history.

However, the story is complicated. The origin of the Slavic peoples' own name — *Slověninъ* in Proto-Slavic — is itself disputed. The most popular scholarly view connects it to the word *slovo* ("word, speech"), meaning roughly "people who speak intelligibly" — as opposed to neighbours whose language was incomprehensible. Another theory links it to *slava*, meaning "glory" or "fame."

So the Slavs' own name for themselves likely meant "the speaking people" or "the glorious people" — and then external peoples, seeing Slavs frequently enslaved, repurposed a similar-sounding word into their term for "slave." It's an ethnic name that got tragically hijacked by history.

**Is using "Slavic" offensive to Czech people?**

No — "Slavic" refers to the language family and cultural group, and Czech people use it themselves without any problem. The discomfort arises specifically with the word "slave" in English, which many Slavic people are aware shares this etymological connection.

Most scholars who have examined the question agreed that nowadays virtually no one associates Slavs with slaves, and that no renaming is needed. As a Slav, the author of one essay noted they bristle at formulations like "the Slavonic peoples had been reduced to a servile state," which collapses a complex, dynamic history into passive victimhood. The irritation, when it exists, is more about how the history is *framed* than about the word "slave" itself.

**What's the non-offensive term for what English speakers mean by "slave"?**

The word "slave" itself is fine and there's no real campaign to replace it. What Slavic scholars and writers sometimes push back against is the implication that Slavic identity is *defined* by having been enslaved — since the Slavs were also conquerors, traders, empire-builders, and scholars. The corrective isn't a new word, but rather richer historical context: acknowledging that Slavic peoples were major actors in medieval history, not merely passive victims. The word for servitude and hard work in Old Slavic itself was *rabota* — from the root *rab-* or *rob-*, related to "orphan." The word "robot," interestingly, comes from this same root.

## Czech Ethnic Identity

The Czech people are a West Slavic ethnic group, their identity shaped by extraordinary historical layering: medieval kingdoms, the Hussite religious revolution (a proto-Protestant movement in the 1400s, a century before Luther), Habsburg rule for nearly 400 years, a German-speaking minority that coexisted and sometimes clashed with Czech speakers, a 20th century that brought Nazi occupation and then Soviet-style communism, and finally the peaceful "Velvet Revolution" of 1989 and the "Velvet Divorce" from Slovakia in 1993.

Czechs tend to be notably secular — the country is one of the least religious in Europe — pragmatic, self-deprecating in their humour, and deeply proud of their cultural heritage (Kafka, Dvořák, Mucha, Havel). A certain wry, dark irony runs through Czech culture, born perhaps from centuries of navigating more powerful neighbours.

## Gifts from Olomouc — The Perfect "Everyday" Souvenir

Your instinct for everyday, authentic local products is exactly right. Here are the best options:

**Olomoucké tvarůžky (Olomouc cheese)** — This is *the* definitive Olomouc gift. It is basically the only Czech original cheese that has survived over generations of producers and is still economically viable, with a history of more than 500 years. It's almost without any fat, making it a very low-calorie food with high protein value. It's a small, pungent, ripened curd cheese with an intensely funky aroma — think a very assertive washed-rind. It's inexpensive, utterly local, and comes in vacuum-packed formats that travel reasonably well. Since 2010, "Olomoucké tvarůžky" has been registered as a Protected Geographical Indication by the EU — so it's genuinely unique to the region. Fair warning about the smell, though — it's famous for it.

**Hanácká kyselka mineral water** — Hanácká kyselka is a natural mineral water from the heart of sunny, fertile Moravia, with a tradition dating back to 1854, collected in the region near Přerov and known for its unique combination of minerals and gentle natural carbonation. It's an "everyday" drink in the region, the way Badoit or Evian is in France, but completely local to Moravia. Inexpensive, distinctive, and something people genuinely use.

**Local Moravian wine** — South Moravia is a serious wine region, and a bottle of local Moravian *Welschriesling* or *Pálava* (a unique Moravian white grape variety) is the kind of thing you can pick up at any supermarket or wine shop for a few euros and which will be genuinely appreciated.

**Tvarůžky-flavoured products** — Beyond the cheese itself, the region now produces tvarůžky spreads, dips, and even chocolate-covered tvarůžky pralines, which are a genuinely fun local novelty for the adventurous recipient.

**Slivovice (plum brandy)** — A small bottle of Moravian slivovice from a local producer is the regional spirit, deeply embedded in Moravian culture, and quite different from the mass-produced versions. It's the equivalent of bringing back a good Calvados from Normandy.

The tvarůžky cheese is probably your closest equivalent to *fleur de sel* — inexpensive, deeply local, genuinely used in everyday cooking and eating, and something people simply can't get elsewhere. It will spark a conversation every single time.
