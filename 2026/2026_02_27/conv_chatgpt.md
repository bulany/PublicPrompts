# Response 1
These are beautiful and thoughtful questions — and very fitting for Ramadan, which is itself a time of reflection on revelation, history, and shared roots.

Let’s walk through this step by step.

---

## 1. Psalm 23 and King David — When Did He Live?

Psalm 23 (“The Lord is my shepherd…”) is traditionally attributed to **King David**.

### 📅 When did David live?

Most historians place David around:

> **c. 1000 BCE** (about 1000 years before Jesus)

Jesus is usually dated to around:

> **c. 4–6 BCE to 30 CE**

So David predates Jesus by roughly **1000 years**.

That means Psalm 23 was already ancient scripture by the time Jesus lived.

---

## 2. Who Was “The Lord” That David Was Talking About?

When David says “The Lord is my shepherd,” he is referring to:

> **YHWH** (often written Yahweh) — the covenant God of Israel.

In Hebrew, Psalm 23 begins:

> *“YHWH ro’i”* — “YHWH is my shepherd.”

The French version you love — *“L’Éternel est mon berger”* — reflects a Jewish tradition of avoiding pronouncing the divine name. Instead of saying “Yahweh,” many traditions substitute:

* “Adonai” (Lord)
* “HaShem” (The Name)
* In French Bibles: **“L’Éternel”** (The Eternal)

You’re perceptive: *“The Eternal”* emphasizes transcendence and abstraction — less anthropomorphic than “Lord.”

---

## 3. Were They “Christians” Back Then?

No.

Before Jesus:

* The people were **Israelites**
* Later known as **Jews**
* Their religion is what we now call **Judaism**

The word “Christian” only appears after Jesus’ death.

The first followers of Jesus were:

* Jews who believed Jesus was the Messiah.
* They did not think they were starting a new religion.
* They saw themselves as faithful Jews.

The term “Christian” appears in the 1st century CE (in Antioch).

---

## 4. The Shared Roots: Judaism, Christianity, Islam

Yes — these three religions are often called:

> **The Abrahamic religions**

Because they all trace spiritual ancestry to **Abraham** (Ibrahim in Islam).

Here’s a simplified “family tree”:

```
                Abraham (c. 2000 BCE)
                       |
                ------------------
                |                |
             Isaac             Ishmael
                |                |
            Israelites        Arab peoples
                |                |
            Judaism            Islam
                |
            Christianity
```

### Judaism

* Begins with Abraham.
* Develops through Moses and the covenant at Sinai.
* David becomes king (~1000 BCE).
* Scriptures: Hebrew Bible (Tanakh).

### Christianity

* Emerges from Judaism (1st century CE).
* Believes Jesus is the Messiah and Son of God.
* Accepts Hebrew Bible + New Testament.

### Islam

* Emerges 7th century CE in Arabia.
* Prophet: **Muhammad** (c. 570–632 CE)
* Accepts Abraham, Moses, David, Jesus as prophets.
* Final revelation: Qur’an.

---

## 5. Is King David Known in All Three Religions?

Yes.

### In Judaism

David is:

* The great king of Israel.
* Author of many Psalms.
* Ancestor of the future Messiah.

### In Christianity

David is:

* King of Israel.
* Ancestor of Jesus.
* A prophetic figure pointing toward Christ.

### In Islam

David (Dāwūd) is:

* A prophet.
* A king.
* Recipient of a revealed book called the **Zabur** (often associated with the Psalms).

So yes — David is revered in all three traditions.

---

## 6. Is There an Arabic Version of Psalm 23?

Yes.

Arabic-speaking Christians use Arabic translations of the Bible.

Psalm 23 in Arabic begins:

> الرب راعيّ فلا يعوزني شيء
> *Al-rabb rāʿiyya fa-lā yuʿwizunī shay’*
> “The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.”

Muslims, however, do **not** use the Psalms liturgically in worship. They believe the original Psalms were revealed but that the Qur’an is the final, preserved revelation.

So:

* Psalm 23 is deeply important in Judaism and Christianity.
* In Islam, David is respected, but the biblical Psalms are not central in worship.

---

## 8. Major Branches of Christianity

Here’s a simplified split timeline:

### 1️⃣ Early Christianity (1st–4th century)

Unified but developing doctrine.

### 2️⃣ 1054 – The Great Schism

Split between:

* **Roman Catholic Church**
* **Eastern Orthodox Church**

Main issues:

* Authority of the Pope.
* Theological wording about the Trinity.
* Cultural/political differences (Rome vs Constantinople).

### 3️⃣ 1517 – Protestant Reformation

Triggered by **Martin Luther**

Leads to:

* Lutheran
* Calvinist/Reformed
* Anglican
* Later Baptists, Methodists, etc.

So today Christianity has three major families:

| Catholic                  | Orthodox                   | Protestant                |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Pope as leader            | No single leader           | No single leader          |
| Strong sacramental system | Similar sacramental system | Varies widely             |
| Developed saint tradition | Strong saint tradition     | Often reduced or rejected |

---

## 9. Saints — What’s Going On?

Great question.

### Are feast days the day they died?

Usually yes.

Traditionally, a saint’s feast day is:

> The day of their death — seen as their “birthday into heaven.”

### Who decides?

In Catholicism:

* The Vatican (under the Pope) canonizes saints.
* A process of investigation takes place.

But here’s the key:

There are different calendars.

* The Roman Catholic Church has a universal calendar.
* Local dioceses can add local saints.
* Orthodox churches have their own calendars.
* Anglican churches (Church of England) have their own commemorations.
* Protestant churches often reduce or reinterpret saint days.

So yes — there is central coordination **within** Catholicism, but not across all Christianity.

---

## 10. Why Do France and Australia Have Different Saints on the Same Day?

Because:

* Catholic calendar ≠ Anglican calendar.
* Local churches emphasize local saints.
* Some saints are optional memorials.
* Some churches follow older liturgical calendars.

For example:

* Saint Valentine (14 Feb) is widely recognized.
* But many other saints are only regionally important.

The astronomical clock in **Olomouc** reflects the Catholic liturgical tradition of Central Europe.

---

## 11. A Bigger Perspective

What you’re noticing is something profound:

Judaism, Christianity, and Islam are not three unrelated religions.

They are historically layered traditions that:

* Share Abraham.
* Share belief in one God.
* Share many prophets.
* Diverge mainly on:

  * Who Jesus is.
  * Whether revelation continued after him.

You could think of it as:

```
Judaism → Christianity → Islam
        (branch)        (branch from Abrahamic line)
```

But each sees itself as the faithful continuation of Abraham’s faith.

---

## 12. One Final Thought

Your appreciation for “L’Éternel” touches something deep.

In:

* Judaism → God is utterly one and transcendent.
* Christianity → God is one but revealed in Trinity.
* Islam → God (Allah) is absolutely one (tawḥīd).

And all three would resonate with calling God:

> The Eternal.
