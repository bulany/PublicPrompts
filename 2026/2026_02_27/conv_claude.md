# Response 1
What a wonderful set of questions — and how fitting to be exploring these during Ramadan, when all three of these traditions are in a sense "in the air." Let me work through everything.

---

## King David, Psalm 23, and the Timeline

Yes, Psalm 23 is in the Hebrew Bible (what Christians call the Old Testament), and King David almost certainly predates Jesus. David is traditionally dated to around **1000 BCE**, meaning he lived roughly **1,000 years before Jesus Christ**. That's not a vague estimate — it's fairly well-established historically and archaeologically, though the exact dates shift a little depending on the scholar.

So when David wrote "The Lord is my shepherd," he was absolutely not talking about a Christian God. He was talking about **YHWH** (often rendered Yahweh or Jehovah), the God of the ancient Israelites — the people we now call **Jews**. They wouldn't have called themselves Christians (that word comes from "Christ," which comes from the Greek for "messiah," and the whole concept hadn't happened yet). They called themselves the **Children of Israel**, or Israelites, or later, Hebrews. The religion was what we now call **Judaism**, though they would simply have called it their covenant with God.

Your instinct about "L'Éternelle est mon berger" is beautiful and theologically interesting — the Hebrew name YHWH is so sacred that it is traditionally not spoken aloud at all. Jews substitute "Adonai" (Lord) when reading scripture. The idea of translating it as "The Eternal" captures something real: YHWH carries connotations of pure being, existence itself, the "I am that I am." So the French translation you love is actually quite philosophically faithful to the original Hebrew spirit.

---

## The Abrahamic Family Tree

You've heard the term "Abrahamic religions" — that's the right term, not "brotherhood of Abraham" exactly, but close. Here's the family tree:

**Abraham** (traditionally around 2000 BCE, though this is more mythological-historical than precisely dateable) is considered the common ancestor of all three faiths. He made a covenant with God, and his two sons are the key branching point:

- His son **Isaac** (by his wife Sarah) is the ancestor of the **Israelites → Judaism → Christianity**
- His son **Ishmael** (by Hagar, Sarah's servant) is considered the ancestor of the **Arab peoples → Islam**

So the three religions share the same root patriarchs. Abraham, Isaac, Jacob (Israel), Moses, and yes — **King David** — are all figures revered across all three.

In Islam, David is known as **Daoud** (داود) and is considered a prophet. The Psalms are even referred to in the Quran — the holy book of Psalms is called the **Zabur** and is listed alongside the Torah and the Gospel as one of the divine books. So yes, Psalm 23 has a kind of sacred echo in Islam too, though it isn't liturgically used the way it is in Christianity and Judaism. There are Arabic translations of Psalm 23 — here's the opening:

*"الرَّبُّ رَاعِيَّ فَلاَ يُعْوِزُنِي شَيْء"* — "The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want."

It's read by Arab Christians (there are millions of them, particularly in Lebanon, Egypt, Syria, Palestine), and it would be familiar in that sense to many Arabic-speaking Muslims too, even if not as a devotional text.

## A Timeline of the Three Religions

| Period | Event |
|---|---|
| ~2000 BCE | Abraham — the common ancestor |
| ~1200 BCE | Moses leads the Israelites out of Egypt; receives the Torah |
| ~1000 BCE | King David rules Israel; writes the Psalms |
| ~960 BCE | Solomon (David's son) builds the First Temple in Jerusalem |
| ~6 BCE–30 CE | Life of Jesus of Nazareth |
| ~50–100 CE | The Christian gospels and letters are written |
| 70 CE | Romans destroy the Second Temple; Judaism disperses |
| 313 CE | Christianity becomes legal in the Roman Empire |
| 380 CE | Christianity becomes the *official* religion of Rome |
| 622 CE | Muhammad's Hijra to Medina — the founding moment of the Islamic calendar |
| 632 CE | Muhammad dies; Islam begins its rapid spread |

## The Branches of Christianity

Christianity began as a Jewish sect in Roman-occupied Judea and spread around the Mediterranean. The splits happened in stages:

**The Great Schism (1054 CE)** — The first and most fundamental split divided Christianity into:
- **Roman Catholic** (West, centred on Rome, led by the Pope)
- **Eastern Orthodox** (East, centred on Constantinople/now Istanbul, led by a group of Patriarchs — no single head)

The Orthodox churches (Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox, Coptic Orthodox in Egypt, etc.) kept what they saw as the original undivided tradition. They have magnificent liturgy, deep mystical theology, and icons — but no Pope.

**The Protestant Reformation (1517 CE onwards)** — A German monk named Martin Luther nailed his complaints (the "95 Theses") to a church door, objecting to corruption and the selling of indulgences. This cracked the Catholic Church wide open in Western Europe, producing:
- **Lutheranism** (Germany, Scandinavia)
- **Calvinism / Reformed** (Switzerland, Netherlands, Scotland)
- **Anglicanism** (England — Henry VIII split from Rome partly for political reasons, creating the Church of England)
- And eventually hundreds of further branches: Baptists, Methodists, Presbyterians, Evangelicals, Pentecostals, etc.

So the Church of England (Anglican) that you mention in Australia is a Protestant church that split from Rome in the 16th century — it sits somewhere between Catholic and Protestant in style, which is why it sometimes shares saints with Catholics but not always the same calendar.

---

## The Saints — Who Decides, and Why Do the Days Differ?

This is where it gets wonderfully complicated.

The Catholic system of saints developed over many centuries. A **saint's feast day** is indeed most often the day of their *death* — understood as their "birthday into heaven." For the very earliest saints (martyrs, apostles), the dates were fixed by local communities commemorating their deaths, sometimes at the actual grave site. These dates got baked into the calendar over centuries.

The formal process of **canonisation** (officially declaring someone a saint) was centralised by the Pope only gradually — really tightening up from the 12th century onwards. It now involves a rigorous multi-stage investigation: first "Venerable," then "Blessed" (after one verified miracle), then "Saint" (after a second miracle). So the Pope does serve as a central authority — but he's working with a calendar that already had centuries of locally-accumulated feast days in it.

The reason for discrepancies between Catholic, Anglican, and Orthodox calendars:

**Different calendars were set before the splits.** When the Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholics divided in 1054, they carried their existing calendars with them and continued developing them independently. When Anglicanism broke from Rome in 1534, it took the Catholic calendar but then reformed it — removing many saints, adding English ones, and shifting emphasis. After the Reformation, the Catholic Church also cleaned up its own calendar significantly, especially in 1969 when Pope Paul VI revised it heavily, removing saints whose historical existence was doubtful (Saint Christopher famously lost his universal feast day then, though he's still venerated locally).

On top of all this, there's the **Julian vs. Gregorian calendar** issue. The Orthodox churches largely kept the old Julian calendar, which is now 13 days behind the Gregorian calendar most of the world uses. This is why Russian Orthodox Christmas is on January 7th — it's the same date in Julian reckoning.

The Olomouc astronomical clock you mention is a wonderful example of a **local** Catholic tradition — Czech saints appear on it that wouldn't appear on a French or Australian Anglican calendar. Saint Valentin aligning is a happy coincidence of a universally famous saint, but many of the others would be regional Czech/Moravian figures.

So to answer your last question directly: **no, there is no single central coordinating authority across all of Christianity.** The Pope coordinates for Catholics (over 1 billion people). The Orthodox Patriarchs coordinate loosely for their churches. The Anglican Communion has the Archbishop of Canterbury as a kind of honorary head but with no binding authority. And Protestant denominations are largely independent. Each tradition maintains its own calendar, and they overlap considerably (especially for the early martyrs and apostles that all traditions share) but diverge significantly for later saints.

It's a beautiful, sprawling, human mess — which in some ways reflects how these traditions grew organically from the same deep root over three thousand years.
