# Response 1
Ramadan is a beautiful time to reflect on these questions! You have touched on a fascinating web of history, theology, and language. Let’s unravel all of this step-by-step, starting with King David and moving through the history of the major religions and the Catholic saints.

### 1. King David, "The Lord," and the First "Christians"
You have great intuition regarding the French translation of Psalm 23. *"L'Éternel est mon berger"* is famously used in the French Protestant translation (the Louis Segond Bible). It beautifully captures the original Hebrew word for God: **YHWH (Yahweh)**. This name is derived from the Hebrew verb "to be" ("I am who I am"), so translating it as "The Eternal" is a brilliant way to capture the abstract, infinite nature of God.

**The Timeline:**
King David absolutely predates Jesus. Historians date King David’s reign to roughly **1000 BCE**. This means David lived about **1,000 years before Jesus was born**.

**Who were they, and who was "The Lord"?**
Because David lived a millennium before Christ, the people of his time were absolutely not called Christians. They were **Israelites** (or Hebrews, and later Judeans, from which we get the word "Jews"). 
The "Lord" David was writing about was **Yahweh**, the God of Israel. The term "Christian" literally means "follower of the Christ" (Christ being the Greek translation of the Hebrew word *Messiah*, meaning "anointed one"). Therefore, the word "Christian" didn't exist until after Jesus's death; according to the Bible, the followers of Jesus were first called "Christians" in the city of Antioch around the year 40 CE.

### 2. The Brotherhood of Abraham (The Family Tree)
Yes, Judaism, Christianity, and Islam share the exact same historical roots. They are collectively known as the **Abrahamic Religions** because they all trace their spiritual (and often literal) lineage back to one man: **Abraham**, who lived around 2000 BCE in what is now modern-day Iraq/Syria.

Here is the "Family Tree" and timeline of how they relate:

*   **Abraham (c. 2000 BCE):** He had two famous sons.
    *   **Isaac:** The ancestor of the Israelites (Judaism and Christianity).
    *   **Ishmael:** The ancestor of the Arab peoples (Islam).
*   **Moses (c. 1300 BCE):** He led the Israelites out of Egypt and received the Torah (the Law), establishing the foundation of **Judaism**.
*   **King David (c. 1000 BCE):** The great King of Israel and author of the Psalms. He is revered by all three religions.
*   **Jesus (c. 4 BCE – 30 CE):** Born into Judaism. His followers believed he was the prophesied Messiah, leading to the birth of **Christianity**.
*   **Muhammad (c. 570 CE – 632 CE):** Born in Arabia. He received the Quran, leading to the birth of **Islam**.

### 3. Psalm 23 in Arabic and Islam
**King David is highly revered in all three religions.** In Islam, he is known as the Prophet **Dawud**. 
In the Quran, it is stated that God gave Dawud a holy book called the **Zabur**, which translates to the *Psalms*. 

Are the Psalms read by Muslims? Yes and no. Muslims deeply respect Prophet Dawud and acknowledge that the original Psalms were divine revelations. However, mainstream Islamic theology holds that the original texts of the Bible and the Psalms were altered or corrupted by humans over centuries (a concept called *tahrif*). Therefore, while Muslims revere the *concept* of the Psalms, they do not generally recite Psalm 23 as part of their religious practice.

However, there **is** an Arabic version of Psalm 23! It is read passionately by millions of Arabic-speaking Christians across the Middle East (in Egypt, Lebanon, Syria, etc.). In Arabic, the beautiful first line is: 
***"Ar-Rabb ra'iyy, fala yu'wizuni shay'"*** (الرَّبُّ رَاعِيَّ، فَلَا يُعْوِزُنِي شَيْءٌ), which translates to "The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want."

### 4. The Branches of Christianity (A Quick Timeline)
To understand Christianity from the outside, it helps to see how it splintered over 2,000 years. 

*   **The Early Church (1st Century – 1054 CE):** For the first thousand years, Christianity was mostly unified, though divided culturally between the Latin-speaking West (Rome) and Greek-speaking East (Constantinople/Istanbul).
*   **The Great Schism (1054 CE):** The Church officially split in two.
    *   **The West** became the **Catholic Church** (led by the Pope in Rome).
    *   **The East** became the **Eastern Orthodox Church** (Russian Orthodox, Greek Orthodox, etc., with no Pope).
*   **The Protestant Reformation (1517 CE):** A German monk named Martin Luther protested against corruption in the Catholic Church, leading to a massive split in Western Europe. This created **Protestantism** (Lutherans, Baptists, Presbyterians, etc.), who rejected the authority of the Pope and the veneration of saints.
*   **The Church of England / Anglicanism (1534 CE):** King Henry VIII of England wanted an annulment from his wife. The Pope said no, so Henry broke away and started his own church, making the English monarch the head. This is the root of the Anglican Church (which is big in Australia, the UK, and America as the Episcopal Church).

### 5. Catholic Saints and the Calendar Discrepancies
Now, regarding your fascinating observations about the Astronomical Clock in Olomouc, France, and Australia!

**How do Saints' days work?** 
In Catholicism, a "Feast Day" is indeed usually the day the saint died. The Church considers this their "heavenly birthday"—the day they entered heaven. 

**Who decides?** 
In the Catholic Church, the Pope has the final say. There is a Vatican department called the *Dicastery for the Causes of Saints* that rigorously investigates miracles and history before someone is officially declared a saint (canonization).

**Why are the days different depending on where you are?**
You hit the nail on the head: **there is no single central authority coordinating all of this globally across all branches.** 

1.  **Universal vs. Local Catholic Calendars:** The Vatican issues a "General Roman Calendar," which lists the major saints everyone celebrates worldwide. However, local regions are allowed to add their own local saints. The Olomouc clock displays Czech saints. In France, the calendar is full of early French martyrs and bishops who are highly culturally relevant to the French (and in France, this morphed into a secular tradition where everyone wishes you a "Bonne Fête" on the day associated with your first name!).
2.  **The Church of England vs. Catholicism:** When the Church of England broke away in the 1500s, they threw out a lot of the Catholic calendar. Over time, they created their own calendar of "commemorations." If an Australian Anglican church is celebrating a saint on a certain day, it might be a post-Reformation English figure (like a famous priest or martyr from the 1800s) who the Catholic Church in France doesn't even recognize as a saint! 
3.  **Too Many Saints:** There are over 10,000 named saints. Because there are only 365 days in a year, most days actually have *dozens* of saints assigned to them. Different calendars simply choose to highlight different ones based on who is most important to that specific country or denomination.

So, when you see different saints on the same day in France and Australia, you are witnessing the direct result of the 16th-century Protestant Reformation and local cultural history!
