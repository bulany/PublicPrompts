# 1. The “dia-” in *diarrhoea*

You’re right: Greek **διά (dia)** means *through* or *across*.

In **diarrhoea**:

* **dia** = through
* **rhein** = to flow
  → “flowing through”

Other common words with **dia-** meaning “through/across”:

* **Diagram** (via Greek *diagramma*)
  Literally “a drawing through” → something marked out by lines across a space.

* **Diameter**
  “Measure across” a circle.

* **Dialogue**
  “Through speech” → exchange of words.

* **Diagnosis**
  “Knowing through” → understanding through examination.

* **Dialect**
  Speech flowing “through” a region.

Notice something important:
Greek roots often drift metaphorically. “Through” becomes:

* across space → diameter
* through speech → dialogue
* through examination → diagnosis
* flowing through → diarrhoea

That flexibility is why etymology sometimes feels abstract.

---

# 2. The “rrh” and the mysterious “oe”

### Why two r’s?

Greek **rhein** (to flow) gives English the combining form:

* **-rrhea** (US spelling)
* **-rrhoea** (UK spelling)

The double **r** preserves the original Greek root when prefixes attach.

You see it in:

* haemorrhage
* rhinorrhea
* seborrhea

---

## The “oe” — what is it?

Yes — it’s related to a **digraph**.

A **digraph** is simply:

> Two letters representing one sound.

Examples in English:

* **th** (think)
* **sh** (ship)
* **ph** (phone)
* **oe** (in older Greek/Latin spellings)

In British English, **oe** often represents a long “ee” sound that historically came from Greek **οι** or Latinized forms.

Common words with **oe**:

* Oestrogen (US: estrogen)
* Oesophagus (US: esophagus)
* Manoeuvre (US: maneuver)
* Phoetus (older; now fetus)

So your brain wants to type “e” after “h” because:

* English words rarely use “oe”
* Modern spelling simplified many of them
* American spelling drops the “o”

Your fingers are following statistical probability.

If you mentally link:

> diarrh**oe**a → oesophagus → oestrogen → manoeuvre

you build a pattern cluster.

---

# 3. Rhythm vs Diarrhoea

Now this is interesting.

## Rhythm

From Greek **rhythmos** = measured flow.

Notice the shared root:

* **rhein** = to flow
* **rhythmos** = regulated flow

So:

* diarrhoea = uncontrolled flow
* rhythm = patterned flow

They are cousins conceptually.

Why is *rhythm* hard to spell?

Because it:

* starts with **rh** (Greek marker)
* has no traditional vowel between “thm”
* breaks common English phonetic expectations

It’s almost entirely consonant skeleton.

Mnemonic people use:

> Rhythm Helps Your Two Hips Move.

# 4. Vocabulary size — how many English words exist?

This depends on how you count.

The **Oxford English Dictionary** contains:

* ~600,000 words (historical + current)

Modern active English?
Maybe 170,000–200,000 current words.

But…

### Average vocabulary size

* Average adult native speaker:
  ~20,000–35,000 words understood
* Active daily use:
  maybe 2,000–5,000
* Most daily speech:
  often <1,000 words

So yes — we operate inside a tiny island of the “wordosphere”.

This is called:

* **Active vocabulary** — words you use.
* **Passive vocabulary** — words you understand but don’t use.
* **Lexicon** — your total word inventory.

Reading expands passive vocabulary first.
Over time, some migrate to active use.

That’s why people like **Akala** advocate reading:

* It increases lexical exposure.
* It increases conceptual diversity.
* It increases expressive range.

Language shapes thought range.

# 7. Your misspellings

### “reccommend”

Correct: **recommend**

Why tricky?

Because:

* Prefix: **re-**
* Root: **commend**

The double consonant belongs to the root:
com**m**end

But only one **c**.

So:
re + commend → recommend

Your brain overgeneralizes doubling.

---

### “imaginitively”

Correct: **imaginatively**

Break it:

imagine
→ imaginative
→ imaginatively

The base is “imagine”, not “imaginit”.

Your brain is trying to create symmetry.
