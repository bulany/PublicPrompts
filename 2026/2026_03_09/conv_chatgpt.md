I heard a story about a man named Ben Patrick (aka "The Knees Over Toes Guy"). After a long battle with knee problems and pain he eventually worked out a system of training that really worked for him, and as I heard how he told it, one day a friend suggested that he might be able to dunk a basketball, so he tried it and he succeeded. It wasn't really one of his explicit goals but I think he was very happy with this result. One exercise he encourages is called the "nordic curl", which I've heard him call "the lower body pull up". 
I have tried this but it seems it requires an immense amount of strength (that I don't have) to overcome the initial lying down position. Ben has suggested various regressions that I have attempted but I don't always have the right equipment for these although I can usually find a way to anchor my feet to at least try the curl.
I typically go through a phase where I try this everyday and don't seem to improve much. I've heard its good to maybe only train a few days a week but I find if I don't do something everyday I quickly forget and fall out of the routine.
How much progress do you think one could make toward the nordic curl in a month and could you suggest a plan that I might be able to work toward this?
How many months for one rep of a full nordic curl? How can I build this kind of training into my everyday busy workday and lifestyle?
What do you think about the difference between explicitly aiming for a goal and not... like if I said to myself "I really want to be able to dunk a basketball" and then after a year of trying, if I couldn't do it I think I would feel demoralised... yet Ben Patrick sort of took a different approach I think... a less direct approach? But honestly I think this was probably a really efficient approach even though it didn't seem as direct.

# Response 1
Your intuition about this exercise—and about goals—is actually very close to how strength coaches think about training. The **Nordic curl** is legitimately one of the hardest body-weight strength movements there is. When Ben Patrick calls it the *“lower body pull-up”*, he’s not exaggerating. Most physically active adults **cannot do even one strict rep**.

Let’s break your questions down into a few useful parts.

---

# 1. Why the Nordic Curl Feels Impossible at First

The difficulty comes from the **strength curve**.

At the start (when your body is horizontal) your hamstrings must produce **very high torque**. This is why even strong athletes struggle.

Important facts:

* Even many professional athletes **cannot do a strict Nordic curl**
* It requires **extreme eccentric hamstring strength**
* The start position is the hardest part

So if you cannot do it yet, you’re exactly where most people are.

---

# 2. Realistic Progress Timeline

This varies hugely, but a reasonable expectation:

| Training level         | Time to first rep |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- |
| Beginner               | **6–12 months**   |
| Moderately trained     | **3–6 months**    |
| Very strong hamstrings | **1–3 months**    |

In **1 month** the most realistic progress is:

* slower eccentric descent
* better control
* reduced assistance
* improved hamstring strength

Those are **big wins** even without a full rep.

---

# 3. Why Doing It Every Day May Be Slowing You Down

Hamstrings respond strongly to **recovery**.

Doing hard Nordics daily often leads to:

* constant soreness
* nervous system fatigue
* stalled strength gains

Most strength programs use:

**2–3 sessions per week**

But your routine instinct (doing *something* every day) is good. We just need to structure it.

---

# 4. A Practical “Daily Habit” Plan

Instead of *hard training daily*, do **light practice daily + heavy work occasionally**.

### Daily (2 minutes)

Grease the groove.

Do **one very easy variation**:

* assisted Nordic with hands
* band-assisted Nordic
* partial range

Example:

```
2 sets
3–5 slow reps
stop far from failure
```

Think **practice**, not workout.

---

### 3x per week (strength session)

Do the real work.

Example:

**Session**

1️⃣ Nordic eccentrics
5 reps × 3 sets
Lower slowly (5 seconds)
Push up with hands

2️⃣ Assisted concentric
3 reps × 3 sets

3️⃣ Hamstring accessory

* hip bridge or hip thrust
* hamstring curl
* reverse nordic

---

# 5. Simple Home Setup

You only need:

* sofa or bed
* partner
* barbell
* heavy furniture

Feet anchored under something sturdy works perfectly.

A couch works surprisingly well.

---

# 6. The Progression Ladder

Work down this ladder over months.

1️⃣ **Eccentric only** (slow lowering)
2️⃣ **Hands assisting** on the way up
3️⃣ **Band assisted** full reps
4️⃣ **Partial range unassisted**
5️⃣ **Full Nordic curl**

Most people spend **months in steps 1–3**.

---

# 7. Signs You’re Getting Stronger

Progress doesn't always show as a rep yet.

Look for:

* slower descent
* less hand push
* less band assistance
* deeper range before collapsing

These are real strength gains.

---

# 8. The Ben Patrick Goal Philosophy

Your observation about goals is really interesting.

Many elite coaches use **process goals** instead of **outcome goals**.

Outcome goal:

> “I want to dunk a basketball.”

Process goal:

> “I will train knees and hamstrings consistently.”

Outcome goals are motivating but risky.

If you miss them → discouragement.

Process goals are **stable**.

This is close to the philosophy of:

* Atomic Habits by James Clear
* many elite training systems

You focus on **daily actions** instead of the result.

---

# 9. Why Ben Patrick’s Approach Works

Ben Patrick tends to train **general athletic capacity**:

* tibialis strength
* hamstrings
* knees over toes
* mobility
* jumping

These create a **system effect**.

Then suddenly a surprising outcome appears:

> “Oh wow, I can dunk.”

The goal appears **as a side effect of capacity**.

This is actually a very powerful training philosophy.

---

# 10. How to Fit This Into a Busy Workday

The trick is **tiny frictionless habits**.

Example:

Morning:

```
3 nordic eccentrics
```

Afternoon break:

```
3 assisted reps
```

Evening:

```
2 slow negatives
```

Each takes **30 seconds**.

Over a year that is **1000+ practice reps**.

---

# 11. One Powerful Mindset Shift

Instead of:

> “Can I do a Nordic curl?”

Think:

> “Can I get **1% stronger hamstrings today**?”

After 6–12 months the rep usually appears almost accidentally.

Just like the dunk story.

---

✅ **Summary**

* Nordic curls are *extremely* hard
* 6–12 months for a first rep is normal
* Train **hard 2–3x/week**
* Practice **lightly daily**
* Track progress by **control**, not reps
* Focus on **process instead of outcome**
