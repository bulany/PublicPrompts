# Response 1
These are great questions, and they connect in a really interesting way philosophically. Let me take them one at a time.

## The Nordic Curl: Realistic Progress

The nordic curl is genuinely one of the hardest bodyweight exercises for the posterior chain — most people, even fairly fit ones, can't do a single clean rep. The eccentric (lowering) phase is where you should start, because your hamstrings are significantly stronger in the eccentric than the concentric. A realistic timeline:

**Month 1:** Learn to control the descent even slightly. Going from flopping straight down to having even 2–3 seconds of controlled lowering is real, meaningful progress — even if it doesn't *feel* like it.

**Months 2–3:** Noticeably slower, more controlled descents (5+ seconds). Possibly assisted concentric (pushing up with hands or using a band).

**Months 3–6:** First partial or full assisted rep for most people. Some people with strong hamstring baselines get there faster.

**6–12 months:** A clean, unassisted rep is achievable for most dedicated people. Some take longer and that's fine.

## A Plan You Can Actually Do Daily

Since you've told me you need *something* every day to stay consistent, here's a way to structure that without overtraining. The key insight is that eccentric-focused work is very taxing, so you rotate intensity:

- **3 days/week (hard days):** 3–5 sets of slow eccentric nordic curls (lower as slowly as you can, use hands to get back up). Rest 2 minutes between sets.
- **4 days/week (easy days):** Just the *setup* — anchor your feet, assume the position, do a 3-second isometric hold at the top (squeezing hamstrings hard), then stand up. Maybe 2–3 sets of 5 holds. This takes under 5 minutes, keeps the pattern in your nervous system, and doesn't fatigue you.

The easy days are your habit anchor. They're almost too easy — which is exactly the point. You never skip them because there's nothing to dread.

For equipment, a couch, heavy furniture leg, or a door with a rolled towel underneath can all work as foot anchors.

## Building It Into a Busy Day

The honest answer is: attach it to something you already do. Before your morning coffee. Right after you sit down at your desk and before you start work. After lunch. The exercise itself, even on hard days, takes under 10 minutes. The *decision* to do it is the hard part, so remove the decision by making it automatic — "after X, I do this."

## The Direct vs. Indirect Goal — This Is the Really Interesting One

I think you've identified something genuinely wise about how Ben Patrick operates, and it connects to a broader idea that shows up across athletic training, creative work, and even therapy.

When you aim *directly* at a big, emotionally loaded goal — "I will dunk a basketball" — a few things happen. First, every session that doesn't produce the goal is subtly coded as failure. Second, your identity gets tied to the outcome, so not achieving it feels like a verdict on *you*. Third, you tend to optimize for the goal itself (practicing dunking attempts) rather than the underlying capacities that would actually get you there.

Ben's approach was different. He was obsessed with the *process* — rebuilding his knees, improving his movement, training systematically. The dunk wasn't the goal; it was more like a *symptom* that showed up because the underlying work was solid. When a friend suggested he try it, he did — and it worked. But notice: he wasn't white-knuckling toward it for a year. There was no accumulated disappointment to overcome.

This is sometimes called **process-orientation vs. outcome-orientation**, but I think the deeper point is about *where you put your identity*. Ben clearly identified as someone who does the work, trains intelligently, and trusts the process. The dunk confirmed something, but failing to dunk wouldn't have shaken him, because that wasn't the foundation.

For you and the nordic curl: I'd suggest reframing it as "I am someone who does hamstring work every day" rather than "I am trying to achieve a nordic curl." The curl will come — probably faster than you expect once the eccentric control clicks — but the daily identity is what makes it stick. And if it takes 8 months instead of 4, that's just 4 more months of being someone who takes care of their body. That doesn't feel like failure at all.
