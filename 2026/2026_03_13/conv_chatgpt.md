# Response 1
What that artist describes lines up with several well-studied ideas in psychology: **intrinsic motivation**, **process orientation**, and the **flow state**. Research by people like Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi (flow), Carol Dweck (growth vs. performance mindset), and Edward Deci / Richard Ryan suggests something important:

> Mindset rarely changes through *belief alone*.
> It changes through **repeated experiences that reinforce a different way of engaging**.

In other words, you don’t directly “decide to stop caring about recognition.”
You **design habits that repeatedly reward attention, curiosity, and play** instead of evaluation.

A good trial period is **30 days**. Habit research (e.g., Phillippa Lally) suggests early psychological shifts start appearing after **2–4 weeks** of consistent repetition.

Below is a **practical 30-day routine** specifically designed to shift someone from *outcome focus* → *process focus*. It’s simple, but psychologically deliberate.

---

# A 30-Day “Process Mindset” Routine for Creative Work

## 1. Morning: 3-minute mindset reset

Before drawing or creating anything.

Write **three short sentences**:

1. *What am I curious about today?*
2. *What small thing could I notice better today?*
3. *What would make this session enjoyable regardless of the result?*

Example:

* Curious about: shadow edges on faces
* Notice: shape of eyelids
* Enjoy: making loose messy marks

This primes **curiosity**, which is strongly tied to intrinsic motivation.

---

# 2. The Core Practice (30–45 minutes)

Split the session into **two modes**.

## Mode A — Observation (15 minutes)

Rules:

* Draw **slowly**
* No erasing
* No fixing mistakes
* Focus on **seeing**, not accuracy

Examples:

* contour drawing
* blind contour
* drawing with continuous line

Purpose:

It **disconnects drawing from performance evaluation**.

---

## Mode B — Exploration (15–30 minutes)

Now allow yourself to:

* exaggerate shapes
* simplify
* experiment with marks

Ask:

> “What happens if I do *this*?”

This builds the habit of **play**.

Play is a major component of creative expertise.

---

# 3. The “No Judgement Rule”

For the entire session:

You **cannot judge the drawing**.

Instead you are only allowed to write observations like:

* “The jaw angle surprised me.”
* “I noticed the eyes tilt upward.”
* “Lines got looser after 10 minutes.”

This rewires the brain toward **perception instead of evaluation**.

---

# 4. Post-Session Reflection (2 minutes)

Answer these three questions:

1. What did I notice today?
2. What moment felt most engaging?
3. Did I forget about results even briefly?

If the answer to #3 is **yes**, that's a success.

Not the drawing.

The **moment of engagement**.

---

# 5. Weekly “Recognition Detox”

One day per week:

Do **creative work that nobody will ever see**.

Examples:

* draw in a private sketchbook
* do ugly experiments
* destroy the drawing afterward

This trains the brain to **decouple creativity from audience reaction**.

---

# 6. The “Delayed Evaluation” Rule

Only review drawings **once per week**.

Never the same day.

This prevents the brain from associating drawing with **immediate judgment**.

---

# What changes psychologically (week by week)

### Week 1

You’ll still think about outcomes.

That’s normal.

But you’ll start noticing **small visual details more often**.

---

### Week 2

Something interesting happens:

You occasionally forget about recognition while working.

Short **flow moments** appear.

---

### Week 3

Your drawings often become **more alive** because marks become less controlled.

This is exactly what the artist in your quote described.

---

### Week 4

Your motivation starts shifting:

Instead of asking:

> “Will people like this?”

You start asking:

> “What happens if I try this?”

That question is the **engine of creativity**.

---

# A very important mental shift

Right now your brain may be thinking:

> Recognition will allow me to take my work seriously.

But many prolific artists operate the opposite way:

> I take the work seriously **first**, regardless of recognition.

Recognition becomes a **side effect**, not the driver.

---

# A helpful framing trick

Instead of:

❌ “I want recognition.”

Try:

✅ “I want to become someone who **observes deeply and draws often**.”

Identity-based goals are much more stable.

# Response 2
Yes—there actually *is* a well-known practice used by many artists, writers, and even scientists. It comes from a philosophy that’s over 2,000 years old.

The trick is called **focusing only on what you control** and deliberately releasing attachment to outcomes.

This idea comes from Stoicism and is often called the **“dichotomy of control.”** It was explained clearly by the Stoic philosopher Epictetus.

The core idea:

> Some things are up to you.
> Some things are not.
> Peace and clarity come from focusing only on the first group.

For a creative person the split looks like this:

| In your control     | Not in your control              |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Showing up to draw  | Whether people watch your video  |
| Observing carefully | Whether an algorithm promotes it |
| Experimenting       | Whether critics praise it        |
| Improving skills    | Whether recognition arrives      |

Recognition sits **entirely in the second column**.

When you repeatedly remind yourself of this, the psychological pressure drops because you stop trying to control something that literally **cannot be controlled**.

---

# A simple daily version of the trick

Before creating anything, say (or write):

**“My job is to draw well and explore.
Recognition is not my job.”**

That sentence alone can rewire how you approach work over time.

It sounds trivial, but repetition makes it powerful.

---

# A famous person who practiced this constantly

The Roman emperor and Stoic writer
Marcus Aurelius wrote about this idea repeatedly in his private notebook, later published as the book Meditations.

He reminded himself:

> “Ambition means tying your well-being to what others say or do.”

His solution was to keep returning to **the work itself**.

---

# A modern creative example

A contemporary example is the filmmaker and writer
David Lynch.

He frequently says something very similar:

> Focus on the process.
> The result takes care of itself.

He talks about protecting the **joy of the idea and the act of making**, rather than chasing reception.

---

# Why this trick works psychologically

It reduces **performance anxiety loops**.

When the brain expects evaluation, it activates threat systems:

* self-criticism
* perfectionism
* overthinking

But when attention returns to **actions you control**, the brain shifts into a task-focused state.

This is exactly the state described in research on **flow** by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi.

Flow happens when:

* attention is on the task
* feedback is immediate
* external judgment is absent

Which is basically **drawing purely to observe and respond**.

---

# A stronger version of the trick (used by many artists)

Some creators go one step further.

They define success **only as completing the session**.

Example rule:

> “If I draw for 45 minutes today, I succeeded.”

The drawing itself is irrelevant.

This creates **guaranteed daily success**, which builds motivation instead of anxiety.

---

# Something interesting about recognition

Ironically, the artists who become widely respected often behave **as if recognition is irrelevant**.

Why?

Because that attitude produces:

* more experimentation
* more work
* more authenticity

All things audiences tend to respond to.

But the key psychological shift is:

> Recognition becomes a **side effect**, not a target.

That distinction is extremely powerful.
