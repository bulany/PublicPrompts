# Prompt 1
"Having the right mindset is fundamental because it shapes how you approach the act of drawing before a single mark is made. If the mindset is wrong—focused on performance, accuracy, or proving ability—everything that follows becomes tense and controlled. You start chasing results, judging yourself, and relying on formulas. In that state, you’re no longer truly observing or responding. The right mindset shifts the focus away from outcome and toward engagement. It allows you to be present, curious, and open to what the subject is offering. Instead of trying to force the drawing to succeed, you’re simply responding to what you see and feel. This mindset removes fear, quiets overthinking, and creates the conditions where genuine observation can happen. When that happens, marks become natural rather than calculated, and the drawing begins to carry life and energy. In other words, technique and skill matter, but mindset determines whether you can actually use them. It’s the foundation that everything else rests on."
This is a quote from a portrait artist who, in my opinion, does fantastic work. And he's very prolific, although he certainly isn't "recognised" in the sense that I think everyone who sees his art would agree that its really "good" but he doesn't get very many views on Youtube. I don't think he is really at all bothered by this as he thinks that underneath it all he is motivated by wanting to share what makes him joyful. I can really see the sense in what he is saying here and I want to sort of apply it to myself. But how to change one's mindset? Even this artist agrees it might not be such an easy task considering we might have a lifetimes worth of expectations kind of weighing down on us.
In terms of drawing and most of my creative output I think I'm probably quite focussed on outcomes, specifically "recognition"... I would love to be recognised enough for what I do that say "people take me seriously" or "I can take it seriously" etc
If someone was in a position like me, could you suggest a practical daily routine to try and start addressing and "fixing" this mindset. Ideally I would love a program where after a little while I can really start to observe results and be more convinced to "trust the process". I don't know what is a good trial timeframe: 14 days? A month? Is there any research that suggests anything?

# Response 1
The quote resonates because it's describing something psychologists would recognize as the difference between a **performance orientation** and a **mastery orientation** — and there's actually solid research on this going back to Carol Dweck's work on mindset, and also Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's work on flow states. The core finding is consistent: people oriented toward outcomes and external validation produce more anxious, rigid work and enjoy the process less. People oriented toward curiosity and growth tend to produce better work *and* find it more sustaining. The frustrating part is that knowing this intellectually doesn't automatically shift anything.

On timeframe: the popular "21 days" idea is a myth that got distorted from older self-help literature. The most cited actual study (Phillippa Lally, UCL, 2010) found habit formation takes anywhere from 18 to 254 days depending on the person and the behavior, with an average around 66 days. A more honest answer for something as deeply ingrained as how you relate to recognition is: **one month is enough to notice a shift, not enough to complete one**. Think of it as a trial where you're gathering evidence, not a cure.

Here's what I'd suggest as a practical daily structure, roughly 30-45 minutes total:

---

**Morning (5-10 minutes) — Before you create anything**

Write three sentences only. Not a journal, not a reflection, just three sentences answering: *what am I curious about today in my work?* Not what do I want to make, not what do I want people to think. What genuinely interests or puzzles you right now. This is harder than it sounds at first, because you'll find yourself defaulting to output-thinking ("I want to finish X so I can post it"). When that happens, notice it and redirect. The goal is to locate some genuine curiosity before you open any tools.

**During your creative session — one rule**

Do at least some portion of your work — even 15 minutes — with the explicit commitment that it will not be shared, shown, or evaluated. Ever. Call it a "closed" session. This isn't about secrecy, it's about removing the audience from the room. The artist in the quote is essentially describing what happens when the imagined audience leaves — marks become natural. You're practicing that feeling deliberately.

**Evening (5 minutes) — Process audit, not quality audit**

Instead of looking at what you made and judging it, ask: *was I present? Was I responding to what I saw, or was I trying to force something?* Rate it on a simple 1-3 scale. Don't rate the work. Rate your engagement. Keep a running log of this — even just in your phone's notes app. After 30 days you'll have something genuinely interesting to look at.

---

**One weekly practice worth adding:** spend one session deliberately making something bad. Not ironically bad, not "I'll try and it'll be bad" — intentionally, committedly bad. Ugly portraits, wrong colors, clumsy marks. This directly confronts the performance orientation because there's nothing to perform. A lot of people find this weirdly liberating and a bit uncomfortable, which is exactly the point.

The honest caveat is that wanting recognition isn't a pathology to be fixed — it's a reasonable human desire, and some of the best artists are quite motivated by it. What you're really trying to untangle is whether recognition-seeking is *overriding* your engagement with the work itself, which it sounds like it might be. The goal isn't to stop caring whether people respond to your work. It's to stop needing that response in order to feel like the work was worth doing. That's a meaningful distinction.

Thirty days of this, done consistently, should give you enough data to feel a real difference — not completion, but enough of a shift that you'll have genuine reason to keep going.
