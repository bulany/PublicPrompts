# Prompt 1
"Having the right mindset is fundamental because it shapes how you approach the act of drawing before a single mark is made. If the mindset is wrong—focused on performance, accuracy, or proving ability—everything that follows becomes tense and controlled. You start chasing results, judging yourself, and relying on formulas. In that state, you’re no longer truly observing or responding. The right mindset shifts the focus away from outcome and toward engagement. It allows you to be present, curious, and open to what the subject is offering. Instead of trying to force the drawing to succeed, you’re simply responding to what you see and feel. This mindset removes fear, quiets overthinking, and creates the conditions where genuine observation can happen. When that happens, marks become natural rather than calculated, and the drawing begins to carry life and energy. In other words, technique and skill matter, but mindset determines whether you can actually use them. It’s the foundation that everything else rests on."
This is a quote from a portrait artist who, in my opinion, does fantastic work. And he's very prolific, although he certainly isn't "recognised" in the sense that I think everyone who sees his art would agree that its really "good" but he doesn't get very many views on Youtube. I don't think he is really at all bothered by this as he thinks that underneath it all he is motivated by wanting to share what makes him joyful. I can really see the sense in what he is saying here and I want to sort of apply it to myself. But how to change one's mindset? Even this artist agrees it might not be such an easy task considering we might have a lifetimes worth of expectations kind of weighing down on us.
In terms of drawing and most of my creative output I think I'm probably quite focussed on outcomes, specifically "recognition"... I would love to be recognised enough for what I do that say "people take me seriously" or "I can take it seriously" etc
If someone was in a position like me, could you suggest a practical daily routine to try and start addressing and "fixing" this mindset. Ideally I would love a program where after a little while I can really start to observe results and be more convinced to "trust the process". I don't know what is a good trial timeframe: 14 days? A month? Is there any research that suggests anything?

# Response 1



The artist you are quoting is touching on a profound truth, not just about art, but about human psychology. When we are driven by external validation—the desire for recognition, wanting to be taken seriously, or wanting to prove our worth—our brains operate in a state of high cognitive load and threat detection. We become tight, risk-averse, and critical. 

The paradox of creative work is that **the outcome you desire (recognition for great work) is usually a byproduct of the very mindset you are trying to avoid (losing yourself purely in the process).**

To rewire a lifetime of outcome-dependence, you need a structured, practical approach that forces your brain to experience the joy of the process without the possibility of an outcome. 

Here is a breakdown of the science behind the timeframe, followed by a practical daily routine to help you rebuild your creative foundation.

### The Research: How Long Will This Take?
You asked if 14 days or a month is a good timeframe. 

In popular culture, there is a myth that it takes 21 days to form a habit. However, the most robust research on habit formation comes from a 2009 study by Dr. Phillippa Lally at University College London. Her team found that it takes an average of **66 days** for a new behavior to become automatic, with the range being anywhere from 18 to 254 days depending on the complexity of the habit. 

Since changing a core mindset is complex, 14 days is likely too short to feel a profound change, as the first two weeks will mainly consist of you fighting your own resistance. 

**Recommendation: Commit to a 30-Day "Black Box" Trial.**
Thirty days is long enough to push past the initial frustration and start seeing the intrinsic benefits (reduced anxiety, moments of "flow"), but short enough to not feel overwhelming.

---

### The 30-Day "Process Over Product" Routine

For the next 30 days, commit to a **30- to 45-minute daily session** dedicated *exclusively* to this mindset shift. You can still do your normal creative work outside of this, but this specific window is your psychological laboratory.

#### Rule #1: The "Black Box" Protocol (Crucial)
For this daily exercise, you are forbidden from sharing the results with anyone. No social media, no showing your partner, no putting it in a portfolio. If there is zero possibility of an audience, the part of your brain that craves recognition has nothing to feed on and will eventually quiet down. 

#### The Daily Breakdown:

**Step 1: The Grounding (3 minutes)**
*   **The Action:** Before you pick up a tool, sit at your workspace, close your eyes, and take a few deep breaths. 
*   **The Prompt:** Remind yourself of your intention. Say out loud or in your head: *"I am not here to make a good drawing. I am here to observe. The paper is just a place to record my looking."*

**Step 2: The "Anti-Outcome" Warm-up (10 minutes)**
To break the need for a "pretty" outcome, you must engage in exercises that are physically impossible to make pretty. This forces you to focus entirely on observation. 
*   **Blind Contour Drawing (5 mins):** Pick an object (your hand, a crumpled piece of paper, a plant). Put your pen on your sketchbook. Look *only* at the object. Slowly trace the edges of what you see with your eyes, letting your hand follow. **Do not look at the paper.** The result will look like a chaotic scribble. That means you succeeded.
*   **Non-Dominant Hand Drawing (5 mins):** Switch your pen to your non-dominant hand and draw the same object. Your lack of motor control will completely kill your ability to be a perfectionist, forcing you to just laugh at the process and look at the shapes.

**Step 3: The Curiosity Study (20 minutes)**
Now, switch back to your normal drawing hand, but change the metric of success. 
*   **The Action:** Choose a subject. Instead of trying to "draw the subject," give yourself a microscopic, process-oriented mission. 
*   **Examples of Process Missions:** 
    *   *Today, I will only look at the negative space (the empty shapes around the object).*
    *   *Today, I will only draw the darkest shadows.*
    *   *Today, I will focus entirely on keeping my hand moving smoothly, regardless of accuracy.*
*   **The Goal:** If you catch yourself thinking, "This doesn't look like a coffee mug," gently redirect your brain back to the mission: "I'm not drawing a mug. I'm just tracking how the light hits this curve."

**Step 4: The De-escalation & Journaling (5 minutes)**
How you end the session dictates how you will remember it. Do not evaluate the art. Evaluate the *experience*.
*   **The Action:** Write 2-3 sentences in the margins of your drawing. 
*   **The Prompts:** 
    *   *What was a moment I felt fully engaged?*
    *   *What is one interesting thing I noticed about the subject today that I never noticed before?*
    *   *When did I feel my ego or frustration pop up, and how did it feel in my body?*

**Step 5: The "Letting Go" Ritual**
Close the sketchbook. Walk away. Do not flip back through previous days to see if you are "getting better." Progress in this 30-day challenge is not measured by the quality of the line, but by the lightness of your mind. 

### How to measure success (Trusting the Process)

If you aren't looking for visual improvement, how will you know this routine is working after 30 days? 

Look for these psychological markers:
1.  **Time distortion:** You will start to notice that during your 20-minute Curiosity Study, you completely lose track of time. This is called "Flow," and it only happens when you are process-focused.
2.  **Decreased heart rate/tension:** You will notice your grip on your pencil is looser. Your shoulders won't be as close to your ears. 
3.  **The "Oh, that's interesting" effect:** When you make a mark that is "wrong" (too long, too dark), instead of feeling a flash of frustration or shame, you will start to feel a mild, neutral curiosity. You'll think, "Oops, went too dark there," and simply adjust, without it ruining your mood.

Wanting to be taken seriously as an artist is a perfectly valid and human desire. But as the portrait artist you quoted wisely noted, you cannot *force* greatness by squeezing the pencil tighter. Greatness comes from deep, uninhibited observation. By giving yourself 30 days in a "Black Box" where recognition is impossible, you will remember what it feels like to draw just for the profound joy of seeing.
