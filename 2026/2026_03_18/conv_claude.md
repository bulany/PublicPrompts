## "Do A Kickflip!" — The Tradition

The phrase predates the Berrics by a long way. It belongs to a broader category of spontaneous street heckling that has existed since skateboarding moved out of empty pools and onto public pavement in the late 70s and early 80s. As skating became a visible urban subculture — something you *did in public* — it created this natural friction point with passersby, other skaters, and people hanging out of car windows.

"Tradition" or "ritual" are both good words for it. "Call and response" might be even better, because that's structurally what it is. It's borrowed from the same deep human impulse as jazz musicians trading fours, or a martial artist being asked to demonstrate their form — *show me what you actually are.*

The Berrics series (particularly with guys like Eric Koston doing it) formalised and amplified it, gave it a cinematic version with stakes and rewards, but they were tapping into something already latent in the culture. The reason it resonated so much is precisely because everyone who skates already *understood* the ritual intuitively before they ever saw the video.

**What role does it play in the subculture?**

A few things simultaneously:

- **Initiation test.** Skateboarding has no belts, no formal ranking, no certificate. The kickflip is one of the most universally recognised informal thresholds — it separates people who roll around from people who have *put in the work.* The shout is a spontaneous, low-stakes field test.
- **Tribal recognition signal.** When another skater shouts it, they're not really heckling — they're *acknowledging* you as someone in the same world. It's closer to a nod between motorcyclists on the road. The form is abrasive but the underlying message is "I see you."
- **Celebration of readiness.** The ideal response — someone just *landing it* first try without breaking stride — is beautiful precisely because it shows the skill is genuinely *owned,* not performed under controlled conditions. That's deeply respected in skate culture.

**The most positive framing:**

Forget the car full of laughing people. Strip it back to its essence: someone is asking you, *"Is this thing you love doing actually a part of you yet?"* That's a gift of a question. The ideal response isn't proving something to them — it's demonstrating something to yourself. The confidence you described (being so un-phased you could respond with good humour) is really just a description of genuine skill acquisition. The shout just becomes an occasion to express something you already have.

The most positive version of "Do a kickflip!" is: *an unexpected invitation to play.*

## The Kickflip Itself — Deliberate Practice Protocol

Your 5% success rate and that one magical moment of felt-certainty are both extremely informative. That moment — where you *knew* before you tried — is not mystical. It's your motor system briefly achieving the right internal model. The goal of practice is to make that state more accessible and more stable.

Here's what the research actually says, applied specifically to your situation:

**1. The problem with your current protocol (and how to fix it)**

One kickflip a day is fine for *accountability and not losing ground,* but it is not sufficient for *skill acquisition.* The research on motor learning (Schmidt, Ericsson, Björk) is unambiguous: you need enough repetitions per session to actually enter the learning state — which means your nervous system needs to fail, adjust, and try again *within the same session.* One attempt is a test, not practice.

What you actually want is: **short, frequent, concentrated blocks of attempts, followed by meaningful rest between sessions.** Something like:

- 3–5 days per week, do a *practice block* (not just one attempt) of 10–20 kickflip attempts
- The other days: one filmed attempt (your current protocol) for accountability and observation
- Rest days are not wasted — motor consolidation happens during sleep and rest. This is extremely well documented. Your brain is literally replaying and encoding motor sequences while you sleep.

**2. Variability is your friend, but only after you have a base**

There's a concept called *contextual interference* — mixing up variations of a skill during practice (rolling vs. stationary, different surfaces, with vs. without the doormat) actually produces better long-term retention than blocked practice (doing the same thing the same way every time), even though it feels harder and your success rate during practice drops. The difficulty is the point — the brain works harder to reconstruct the movement each time.

However: you're at 5%. You need *some* consistency first. For now, keep the doormat / stationary attempts. But once you're landing, say, 30–40% consistently in that controlled setting, deliberately introduce variation.

**3. The rhythm insight you had about Luan is genuinely important**

You stumbled onto something motor learning researchers call *temporal structure* — the timing relationships between movement components. Your three-sound breakdown (pop → catch → land) is actually a legitimate way to encode the rhythm of the skill. Musicians use this instinctively: if you can hum the rhythm of a passage before you can play it, you're ahead. Same principle applies here.

What I'd suggest: find the *sound* of a good kickflip from your own footage. On the days where you get a clean one, that audio fingerprint is useful. You can try to reproduce the *rhythm* of the sounds even before you reproduce the visual form.

**4. What to consciously think about vs. autopilot**

Here's the research-backed answer: during the *attempt itself,* you want as close to zero conscious thought as possible. Conscious attention narrows and slows processing; it is the enemy of fast motor execution. This is called *paralysis by analysis* and it's extremely well documented in sports science.

*Before* the attempt: one clear, simple *intention cue* — not a checklist. Something like "wait, then flick level" or just the rhythm in your head. One thing. Then let go.

*After* the attempt: that's when the analysis happens — slow motion review, comparison, mental logging.

The pre-attempt visualisation should be brief (5–10 seconds), full speed or slightly slow, and focused on *feel* not mechanics. See it, feel it, do it. Don't narrate it.

**5. Visualisation — what the research actually says**

Visualisation (mental rehearsal / motor imagery) is one of the most studied interventions in sports psychology. The findings:

- It works best as a *complement* to physical practice, not a replacement
- The most effective form is *first-person, kinesthetic* — you feel the movement from inside your body, not watch yourself from outside (though external perspective has some value for style/form correction)
- Brief and vivid beats long and vague — 30 seconds of sharp, felt imagery is more useful than 5 minutes of fuzzy mental wandering
- It should be *successful execution* you're imagining. Repeatedly visualising failed attempts actually encodes failure. If you can't complete the visualisation successfully, stop and reset.
- You can't really overdo short sessions, but there are diminishing returns. Think of it like a tool you use consistently rather than a magic dose.

**For your specific situation:** visualise Luan's kickflip *and then immediately* visualise yourself doing it with the same rhythm. The first primes the pattern; the second applies it to your own body schema. Do this before your filmed daily attempt. 30 seconds total.

## Your Slow-Mo Analysis of Luan Oliveira

Your breakdown is genuinely excellent and accurate. A few things worth highlighting:

The **preparatory hop** you noticed — that's Luan resetting his weight distribution and timing his approach rhythm. It's also subtly loading his ankles. This is common in high-level kickflips and often completely invisible at full speed.

The **arm swing** (down into the crouch, up into the jump) is doing real mechanical work — it's contributing to vertical momentum through the kinetic chain. Most beginners keep arms stiff or forget about them entirely.

The most important thing you noticed: **his back foot catches the board at the *top* of its arc, while the board is still travelling upward.** This is the signature of a "floaty" high-quality kickflip. Most beginners (and many intermediates) catch the board as it's coming back down, which makes the landing look dropped rather than controlled. The timing of the back foot catch is probably the single highest-leverage technical adjustment you could work toward.

Your description of the front and back feet working as a *juggling team* is perfect. That's exactly the right mental model.

**Luan's front foot flick:** he says he waits. What he's actually doing is completing the pop and getting full height *before* the flick, which means the board has more time to complete its flip at height rather than flipping at knee level on the way up. This is a timing adjustment that is very hard to consciously execute — but knowing it intellectually can inform your visualisation and your slow-mo review.

## First Try Friday — Is There Research Behind It?

The Berrics formalised it, but the underlying idea is very solid and maps onto a real concept in motor learning: **retention testing under transfer conditions.** 

Researchers distinguish between *acquisition* (how well you perform during practice) and *retention/transfer* (how well the skill holds up later, under different conditions, without warmup). These are surprisingly poorly correlated. You can perform well during a practice block and still not have the skill durably encoded.

First Try Friday is essentially a weekly *retention test.* The no-warmup condition is meaningful — it tests whether the skill exists in your nervous system independently, not just as an artifact of you warming up and priming the movement pattern. This is why it *feels* like a purer test of ownership. Because it is.

The research actually suggests that *testing yourself this way regularly* — even when you fail — accelerates learning compared to always practicing under optimal conditions. It's called the *testing effect* and it applies to motor skills as well as memory.

## The Piano Analogy — And How To Actually Use It

Your piano analogy is exactly right and very useful. Fast motor sequences in music and in skateboarding share the same fundamental learning problem: the execution happens *below the speed of conscious thought.* You cannot think your way through a kickflip any more than you can consciously direct each finger movement in a fast scale passage.

The pedagogical solution in both domains is the same:

1. **Slow it down until you can do it consciously and correctly**, then gradually speed it up. For piano this is literal (metronome at 50% tempo). For kickflips — this is harder physically, but you can do it mentally: slow-motion visualisation of the *correct* movement, then full speed execution where you let go.

2. **Isolate the components.** Pianists practice the left hand alone, then the right hand alone, then together. For kickflips: you can practice just the pop without the flick. Just the weight shift. Just the arm movement. Without the board entirely if needed. Mitchie Brusco (whom you mentioned) does exactly this in his teaching — breaking the trick into separable components and drilling them independently before combining.

3. **Find the minimum viable cue.** In music, sometimes a teacher gives you one thing to focus on and that unlocks everything else. For your kickflip — based on your Luan analysis — I'd guess the single highest-leverage cue is: *catch the board before it starts falling.* That timing cue, if you genuinely internalise it, probably reorganises several other things automatically.
