Let's break down your sprawling, wonderful questions into actionable, philosophical, and practical pieces.

### 1. The Origins and Culture of "Do a Kickflip!"
You are spot on about this being a "tribal identity call." Skateboarders have historically been treated as public nuisances. For decades, the things yelled out of car windows at skaters were usually insults, threats, or at best, a mocking "Do a wheelie!" or "Do an ollie!" 

The modern "Do a Kickflip!" phenomenon as a positive, viral game was indeed codified by **Eric Koston and The Berrics** in 2018. Koston drove around Los Angeles yelling "Do a kickflip!" at unsuspecting skaters. If they landed it, he handed them free boards, wheels, or merch out of the window. 

**The most positive framing:** 
Think of it as a spontaneous, real-world shibboleth—a secret handshake. When someone yells "Do a kickflip!", they are temporarily suspending the mundane reality of traffic, commutes, and daily grinds to invite you into a moment of shared joy. It changes the street from a hostile environment into a playground. It says, *"I see you. I know how hard what you are doing is. Show me the magic."* If you reframe it this way, you remove the fear of ridicule. Even a failed attempt, greeted with a smile and a shrug, is a successful participation in the ritual.

### 2. "First Try Friday" & The Science of Skill Acquisition
"First Try Friday" is also a Berrics original concept (dating back to around 2014) where pro skaters are challenged to land a trick completely cold, with zero warm-up. 

In motor learning literature, this touches on a concept called the **"Warm-up Decrement."** When you are learning a skill, you usually need a few practice runs to "groove" your nervous system and calibrate your muscles. True mastery—or *automaticity*—is achieved when a motor pattern is so deeply wired into your implicit memory that the warm-up decrement is practically zero. Being able to do a trick "first try" is the ultimate test of motor consolidation. It means the skill has shifted from the conscious, cognitive part of your brain (which is slow and easily stressed) to the automatic, motor-control centers (which are lightning-fast).

### 3. Deconstructing the Kickflip: Rhythm, "Knife-Spinning," and Luan Oliveira
Your analogy of "knife-spinning" is brilliant. An ollie is jumping over an obstacle; a kickflip is manipulating a lever in multiple spatial planes while you are hovering above it. 

Your breakdown of **Luan Oliveira’s** kickflip is incredibly accurate. What you are noticing in his technique is what skate coaches (like Mitchie Brusco) call the **"Delayed Flick"** or the "Ninja Kick." 
*   **The Flaw of the 5% Skater:** Beginners try to flick the board *while* they pop. This causes the board to spin low to the ground and rocket away. 
*   **The Luan Technique:** His back foot pops the board *aggressively*, and he allows the board to travel upward *before* his front ankle flicks off the very corner of the nose. He is essentially doing a high ollie, and at the absolute apex, his front foot strikes. This is why the board is glued to his back foot on the catch, and why it's leveled out so beautifully.

**The Piano Rhythm Connection:**
You are 100% correct about rhythm. In motor learning, using **Rhythmic Auditory Cues (RAC)** is a proven way to learn complex sequencing. You can't think of the mechanics in real-time—it's too fast. But you *can* internalize a rhythm. Luan's kickflip has a distinct beat: *Pop... (micro-pause)... Flick... Catch... Thud.* 
Next time you practice, don't think about your feet. Try to recreate that *musical beat* with the wood on the concrete. 

### 4. Deliberate Practice & Visualization Protocols
Using deliberate practice to bridge the gap from 5% to 100% requires specific parameters:

*   **Autopilot vs. Conscious Focus:** *Before* the trick, use conscious focus (e.g., "I'm going to delay my flick"). *During* the attempt, switch to pure autopilot. Paralysis by analysis mid-air will cause you to land primo. *After* the attempt, go back to conscious analysis.
*   **Visualization (Motor Imagery):** Sports psychology strongly supports motor imagery. Your brain fires the exact same neural pathways when you vividly imagine a movement as when you execute it. 
    *   *Protocol:* Visualize in both slow-motion (for mechanical breakdown) and real-time (for timing and rhythm). Do it from the first-person perspective (feeling your own feet) and the third-person perspective (watching Luan).
    *   *Dosage:* You don't need much. 2–3 minutes while waiting for the kettle to boil is plenty.
*   **Rest Days:** YES. Skill acquisition happens *between* sessions, particularly during REM and Deep Sleep. Your brain prunes and strengthens neural connections when you rest. Doing it every single day can lead to a plateau. Taking a day off to just ride your board (or do nothing) is when the kickflip actually gets "installed" in your brain.

You mentioned wanting a workflow that kills the heavy video compression, the WhatsApp ping-ponging, and integrates with Obsidian (files over apps).
