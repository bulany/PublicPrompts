## Marcelle Auclair — Life & Work

### The Timeline: Children, the War, and the Books

Here is what we can piece together with confidence. Marcelle Auclair was born on 11 November 1899 in Montluçon and spent part of her childhood and youth in Chile, where her father settled in 1906 — so she left France at age 6 or 7. Returning to France in 1923, she married the writer Jean Prévost — she was 23 or 24 at the time. In 1926 he married Marcelle Auclair with whom he had three children: Michel, Françoise and Alain. They divorced in 1939.

For the children's ages: we know Françoise's exact birthday because she became a well-known actress. Françoise Prévost was born 13 January 1930. The birth dates of Michel and Alain are not in any publicly available source I can find, but given the marriage was in 1926 and they had three children before the divorce in 1939, they were likely born roughly between 1927 and 1933.

So at the time of **the war** (1939–1944):
- Marcelle herself was **39 to 44 years old**
- Françoise was **9 to 14 years old**
- Michel and Alain were likely in a similar range — pre-teen to mid-teen years

It's worth noting that the divorce happened in 1939, the very year France went to war. Auclair left her husband after he humiliated her at a dinner party. Her ex-husband Jean Prévost became a Resistance fighter and was killed on 1 August 1944. So the war years were personally devastating — she was a single mother of three young children and her ex-husband died fighting for France just before liberation.

At the time of **publishing**:
- *Le bonheur est en vous* (1951): Marcelle was **51**, Françoise was **21**
- *La pratique du bonheur* (1956): Marcelle was **56**, Françoise was **26**

This context is quietly significant. She was not writing as a young idealist. She had survived a painful divorce, wartime occupation, the death of her children's father, and was in her early 50s when she put pen to paper about happiness. These weren't abstract ideas.

### The Mystery Book and the Positive Thinking Awakening

This is one of the more intriguing gaps in the record, and I want to be honest with you: **no source I can find names the specific book she discovered.** Her autobiography *Mémoires à deux voix* (1978, co-written with her daughter Françoise) is the most likely place this detail appears, but the full text isn't accessible online.

What we *can* say is that the intellectual context points strongly in one direction. Multiple reviewers compare her directly to Dr. Coué, the French pharmacist and psychologist Émile Coué (1857–1926), whose method of conscious autosuggestion ("Every day, in every way, I'm getting better and better") was an international sensation from around 1920 onward. Critics note that her thinking goes much deeper than Coué's superficial aphorisms, but acknowledge the connection is partially valid.

Coué's major work, *La Maîtrise de soi-même par l'autosuggestion consciente*, was published in 1920. Auclair would have been about 20 or 21. She was still in Chile at this point, where her father was a prominent figure. Her mother's illness would likely have been during this period. Coué's ideas were spreading rapidly in France and internationally, and it's entirely plausible — though not confirmed — that this was the book she passed to her mother.

Other candidates from the New Thought movement that circulated in that era include works by **William James** (*The Will to Believe*, *The Varieties of Religious Experience*) or possibly earlier French thinkers. But the Coué connection is the strongest thread in the published record. The reason she may not have named the book in her writing is interesting to speculate about — perhaps she felt she had transcended or significantly transformed its ideas, or perhaps she wanted to avoid having her work reduced to being "mere Coué."

### Sainte Thérèse d'Avila

This relationship clearly ran very deep and for a very long time. All through her life Marcelle Auclair assiduously studied the writings of Teresa of Avila. She admired in her an essentially "modern" woman: inventive, practical, gallant and intrepid, with tremendous organizational capacities, whose genius permitted her to break through the restrictions of her time.

What drew her to Teresa is revealing: she didn't approach her as a mystic to imitate but as a strong, practical woman who somehow broke through all the constraints placed on her. For a woman who founded *Marie Claire*, raised three children largely alone during a war, and wrote boldly about happiness and the inner life, Teresa must have felt like a kindred spirit across the centuries.

Auclair decided to retranslate the saint's writings and write her life. With characteristic determination, she attempted to obtain the seemingly impossible — an authorization from the Holy See to enter the Carmelite cloisters in Spain — and the permission was granted. On her return to Paris she gave up all her professional obligations and for two years virtually went into retreat, devoting her entire time to the accomplishment of what is no doubt the most vivid existing biography of this great saint.

Her *Vie de Sainte Thérèse d'Avila* was published in 1950 — the year *before Le bonheur est en vous*. This sequencing matters. She immersed herself completely in Teresa, then wrote about happiness. The two projects are deeply connected in her inner life. André Maurois called it a "perfect biography" upon publication. The timing — roughly when she was 48–50 — suggests Teresa became important to her during or just after the war years, when she may have needed exactly that model of a woman finding inner resources under impossible external constraints.

### Obsidian and .txt Files

Good question about the .txt files. Obsidian is built around Markdown and its vault is Markdown-first. It *can* open .txt files — they will appear in the file explorer and you can read and edit them — but it will **not** render them as formatted text (no headers, no bold, no rendered links). They'll appear as plain text. If you want the nice rendered view, the content needs to be in a `.md` file. The good news is that if you have `.txt` files that are *already* plain prose notes, they'll open fine — you just won't get the visual rendering benefits of Obsidian.

Given you're already comfortable editing `.txt` in VSCode and `.md` in Obsidian, a clean approach would be: keep the long-lived plain text archives as `.txt` in VSCode, and create new structured "hero" notes as `.md` in Obsidian. They're both just text — you can always rename a `.txt` to `.md` and it will immediately become Obsidian-native.

A few notes on the design choices:

**Why inline fields for `idea::`, `read::`, `resonance::` etc.** — these sit naturally in the flow of the text, read like labels a thoughtful person might add, and Dataview can query them across your whole vault (e.g. "show me all `idea::` fields from all council members"). They won't look weird to a human or an LLM.

**The "Why She Matters to Me" section** — leave this deliberately personal and write it in your own voice. This is the single most valuable section for LLM context, because it tells the model not just *who she is* but *what she means to you* and *through what lens you see her*. An LLM with that section can adapt its entire tone and framing accordingly.

**The Open Questions section** — keeping your live research questions in the file means the document grows organically. It also helps enormously when you hand the file to an LLM and say "let's work on some of these."

For your other "council" members, you'd use the same template. The `council-role::` field is nice because you can give each figure a distinctive role — guide, contrarian, patron saint of craft, etc. — which again helps LLMs understand *why* they're in your personal pantheon.
