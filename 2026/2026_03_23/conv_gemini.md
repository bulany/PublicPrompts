# Response 1
It is wonderful that you are creating a "Jedi Council" of personal heroes. Marcelle Auclair is a fascinating choice; she seamlessly bridged the worlds of modern journalism (co-founding *Marie Claire*), literature, and deep, practical spirituality. 

Here is a detailed breakdown of the timeline, the mystery book, her connection to Sainte Thérèse d'Avila, and how to structure your notes in Obsidian.

### 1. The Timeline: Motherhood, the War, and Publishing
Marcelle Auclair was born on **November 11, 1899**, in Montluçon, France. When she was around 6 or 7, her father moved the family to Santiago, Chile, where she spent her youth. 

*   **Age when she had children:** She married writer Jean Prévost in 1926 at age 26. They had three children: Michel (born 1927), Françoise (born January 1930), and Alain (born August 1930). Therefore, **she was between the ages of 27 and 30** when she had her children. 
*   **During World War II (1939–1945):** Marcelle and Jean divorced in 1939, right as the war was starting. At the outbreak of the war, **Marcelle was 39 years old**. Her children were very young: Michel was 12, Françoise was 9, and Alain was 9. By the end of the war, she was 45, and her children were teenagers. (Tragically, her ex-husband Jean Prévost became a French Resistance fighter and was killed in the Vercors maquis in 1944).
*   **When she published her books:** 
    *   *Le Bonheur est en vous* was published in **1951**. Marcelle was **51 years old**. Her children were young adults ranging from 21 to 24 years old.
    *   *La Pratique du Bonheur* was published in **1956**. Marcelle was **56 years old**. Her children were 26 to 29 years old.

### 2. The Mystery Book That Healed Her Mother
The book Marcelle found as a young girl in Chile was by **William Walker Atkinson**, an American pioneer of the "New Thought" movement. 

According to historian Sarah Fishman’s recent research on Auclair (*Bridges to Feminism*, Oxford Academic), Marcelle's mother was suffering from a deep, debilitating illness. Marcelle, likely in her early teens at the time, discovered Atkinson's writings. Atkinson asserted that thoughts, like light and sound, consisted of physical waves and vibrations. He taught that people could modify their physical and internal state by reorienting these thought vibrations. 

Marcelle loved the ideas, shared them with her mother, and used them to completely cure her. Auclair later wrote in her autobiography *À la grâce de Dieu* that the moment she and her mother understood that "the will of God is happiness," her mother was never sick again, and her newfound cheerfulness became a blessing to the family. Marcelle likely didn't name the book in her mainstream French publications because Atkinson was a somewhat obscure American esotericist, but his concepts formed the direct foundation of her "positive thinking" philosophy.

### 3. Her Connection to Sainte Thérèse d'Avila
Marcelle published her highly acclaimed biography, *La Vie de sainte Thérèse d'Avila* (The Life of Saint Teresa of Avila), in **1950** when she was **50 years old**. 

Her interest in the Saint stemmed from her childhood in Chile. Growing up immersed in Hispanic culture, she became completely fluent in Spanish and deeply connected to Spanish literature and spirituality (she also translated the works of Federico García Lorca into French). 

Sainte Thérèse d'Avila was the perfect spiritual mentor for Marcelle. Thérèse was a highly practical, active woman—a reformer and a "doer"—but also a profound mystic. This mirrored Marcelle’s own life. Marcelle was a busy mother, an independent woman, and a high-powered Parisian journalist, yet she possessed a profound interior spiritual life. Thérèse’s philosophy that the divine could be found "among the cooking pots" perfectly aligned with Marcelle’s belief that spirituality and happiness are highly practical and belong in everyday life.

### 4. Structuring your "Jedi Council" in Obsidian

To answer your first question: **Obsidian is not built to natively format and render `.txt` files.** While you can force it to view them using community plugins, you are fighting the software. 

**My strong suggestion:** Just change the file extensions from `.txt` to `.md`. A Markdown (`.md`) file is literally just a plain text file. You can still open, edit, and read a `.md` file in VSCode, Notepad, or any basic text editor for the next 20 years just as you have with `.txt` files. However, by naming it `.md`, Obsidian will instantly give you beautiful rendering, linking, and Dataview capabilities.

#### How to use Dataview Inline Fields (Instead of YAML)
If you dislike bulky YAML frontmatter (the metadata block at the very top of a file between `---` lines), Dataview has a fantastic feature called **Inline Fields**. 

You use a double-colon `::` to declare a field anywhere in your text. It reads like a natural sentence or a clean list, remains entirely human-readable, and both Dataview and an LLM will instantly understand the structure.

Here is exactly how I would structure your `Marcelle Auclair.md` file:

```markdown
# Marcelle Auclair
Role:: Author, Journalist, Practical Mystic
Birth_Year:: 1899
Death_Year:: 1983
Lifespan:: 83 years
Nationality:: French

## Why she is on my Council
Marcelle Auclair represents the perfect blend of practical, everyday action and deep spiritual peace. She discovered the power of positive thought as a teenager by reading William Walker Atkinson and used it to cure her mother's illness. 

## Key Milestones
- **1939-1945 (WWII):** Raised three children (Michel, Françoise, Alain) alone through the war after her divorce; her ex-husband died in the Resistance.
- **1950 (Age 50):** Published a biography of her own spiritual hero, Sainte Thérèse d'Avila.
- **1951 (Age 51):** Published *Le Bonheur est en vous*.
- **1956 (Age 56):** Published *La Pratique du Bonheur*.

## Core Philosophy
Marcelle believed that thoughts have physical vibrations. Her core tenet was that "the will of God is happiness."

## Quotes & Notes
> "La joie, l'amour, la santé, l'abondance, la concorde, la paix, le bonheur sont de ce monde."
```

### Why this is perfect for LLMs
If you feed files structured like this into an LLM, the AI easily parses the `Key:: Value` syntax. It immediately grasps the metadata context (Role, Lifespan, Nationality) and then reads your highly personal "Why she is on my Council" section. 

By creating a folder called "Jedi Council" and writing a clean, markdown-based profile for each of your heroes, you can upload that folder to an LLM context window. The AI will instantly understand your personal values, the historical figures you model your life after, and the specific philosophies you want to draw upon when asking it for advice!
