# Response 1
You’re circling a really interesting intersection here—AGI definitions, epistemology (“what counts as knowing?”), and humor built on cultural tension. Let’s unpack it piece by piece.

---

# 🧠 Successors to the Turing Test

The original Turing Test (proposed by Alan Turing in 1950) basically asked: *can a machine convincingly imitate a human in conversation?*

As you said, that’s no longer very satisfying. Modern systems can pass it in many contexts without truly “understanding.”

Here are some notable successors / alternatives:

### 1. **The Total Turing Test**

Extends the original by requiring:

* perception (vision, audio)
* physical interaction
  👉 Moves toward embodied intelligence.

---

### 2. **The Winograd Schema Challenge**

A famous example:

> “The trophy doesn’t fit in the suitcase because it is too big. What is too big?”

Requires commonsense reasoning—not just pattern matching.

---

### 3. **The Lovelace Test**

Named after Ada Lovelace

* Can the AI produce something *truly original* that its creator cannot explain?

---

### 4. **ARC (Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus)**

Proposed by François Chollet

* Tests ability to generalize from small examples
* Designed to resist “benchmark memorization”

---

### 5. **BIG-bench / HELM / MMLU-style evaluations**

Modern large-scale benchmark suites:

* Broad coverage
* Still vulnerable to training-data leakage (as you pointed out)

---

### 6. **Embodied / Agent-based tests**

Think:

* Can an AI operate in the real world?
* Plan, adapt, pursue goals over time?

👉 This is where many researchers now think “real AGI” will be demonstrated.

# 🧩 What kind of humor is this?

You’re combining a few technical humor types:

### 1. **Incongruity Theory**

* Serious AGI discussion → trivial but revealing test (Banksy)

### 2. **Satire / Social Commentary**

* You’re implicitly critiquing:

  * surveillance
  * loss of privacy
  * what “intelligence” really means

### 3. **Reductionism for comedic effect**

* Compressing a complex concept into a single absurd metric

### 4. **Benign Violation Theory**

* The idea (“AI can expose anyone”) is unsettling
* But framed as a joke → safe enough to laugh at

# 🤔 Why you're so tempted to click that Banksy video

This is actually super well-studied psychologically:

### 1. **Information Gap Theory (George Loewenstein)**

* Curiosity = gap between what you know and what you *almost* know
  👉 “Banksy identity” is the perfect unresolved gap

---

### 2. **Mystery + social payoff**

* If you know the answer, you gain:

  * status (“I know something others don’t”)
  * narrative closure

---

### 3. **Pattern completion**

Your brain *hates* unfinished stories:

* Banksy = long-running unresolved narrative
* Your brain wants closure like it wants to finish a song

---

### 4. **Forbidden knowledge effect**

* Feels like something you’re *not supposed* to know
  👉 increases perceived value
