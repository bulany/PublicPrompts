## Successors to the Turing Test

The Turing Test — where a machine tries to convince a human judge it's human in conversation — has been largely retired as a meaningful AGI benchmark. A 2025 pre-registered, three-party Turing test study showed that GPT-4.5 was judged to be the human in 73% of five-minute text conversations, surpassing the 67% rate of real human participants — meeting the researchers' criterion for having passed the test. So yes, you're right that it's been effectively beaten.

The main successors are:

**ARC-AGI** — probably the most talked-about current benchmark. Pure LLMs score 0% on ARC-AGI-2, AI reasoning systems score only single-digit percentages, yet extensive testing shows every task can be solved by humans. It deliberately avoids world knowledge (so pre-training can't cheat) and instead tests abstract pattern recognition. ARC-AGI-1 was unbeaten for roughly five years until December 2024, when OpenAI's o3 model matched human performance — but that same model got a measly 4% on the harder ARC-AGI-2.

**The Tong Test** — a 2024 paper proposed this test where virtual people are assigned randomised tasks testing not only understanding but values — for example, an AI might unexpectedly encounter money on the floor or a crying baby, letting researchers observe what it does.

**Real-world physical tests** — Steve Wozniak proposed a "Coffee Test" where a machine must enter an average American home, find the coffee machine, find the coffee, add water, find a mug, and brew the coffee by pushing the right buttons. Others have proposed earning a university degree by attending classes as a human student would.

## The Technical Name for This Style of Humour

What you're doing is called **bathos** (or comic deflation). It's the technique of suddenly dropping from something grand and abstract down to something mundane and specific. The philosophical question of AGI is enormous; "but can it tell us who Banksy is" is tiny and petty by comparison. That gap is where the laugh lives.

It also has elements of the **reductio ad absurdum** — using a single ridiculous test case to mock a grand claim. And structurally it's a **litmus test joke**, a subcategory where the humour comes from proposing an absurdly narrow, quirky criterion for a sweeping conclusion.

## Why You *Need* to Click That Thumbnail

The psychological phenomenon you're experiencing is called the **need for cognitive closure** — humans are hardwired to feel discomfort with unresolved mysteries and relief when they're solved, even mysteries that have absolutely no practical effect on your life. Banksy's identity is a perfect example because it combines several irresistible psychological hooks: transgression (his art is technically illegal), celebrity, mystique, and long-running suspense. The longer the mystery, the greater the cognitive itch. Neuroscientists link this to dopamine — your brain rewards *resolution* almost as much as it rewards discovery.

There's also a social element: knowing Banksy's identity is a piece of cultural currency. Once it's common knowledge, you don't want to be the last one to know.

## "I Saw What You Did There"

Yes! It's a very established internet meme and phrase. It's used when someone notices a subtle, clever, or self-referential move in what someone else said or did — a hidden joke, a wordplay, an ironic reversal — that could easily go unnoticed by a less attentive audience. The implicit message is: *"I'm smart enough to have caught that."*

Technically, it sits at the intersection of **meta-humour** (humour about humour) and **incongruity recognition** — the pleasure of spotting that two things unexpectedly connect. The joke is partly for the speaker, partly as a social signal of wit.

**A few good examples:**

1. Someone posts a photo of a cat sitting in a bread loaf position with the caption "I am a *cat*-alog of loaves." Someone replies: *"I saw what you did there."* The acknowledgment is itself the punchline.

2. A web developer names their error-handling function `catch22()`. A colleague in a code review writes: *"I see what you did there... and I hate you for it."*

3. A news headline reads: *"Banksy's identity revealed — he was hiding in plain sight."* Someone comments: *"In plain sight. IN PLAIN SIGHT. I see what you did there."*

The beauty of the phrase is that saying it *is* the joke — you're performing your own perceptiveness, which is slightly self-congratulatory, which everyone knows, which makes it affectionate rather than smug when used right.
