## 2. Where the Technology Is At

The good news is that most of the pieces exist. The honest news is that the *integration* at the quality level you're imagining is still a gap.

**What already works well:**

- **Depth cameras + 3D scanning on edge devices.** The iPhone LiDAR and similar sensors can produce a decent 3D mesh of a log in seconds. Libraries like Open3D run on-device. This part is largely solved.
- **AR overlays anchored to real-world geometry.** Apple's ARKit and Google's ARCore do exactly this — they can lock a line to a physical surface and hold it as you move. The quality is genuinely good outdoors on a flat-ish surface.
- **Ruggedized smart glasses.** The RealWear Navigator and Vuzix M400 are built for industrial use — dusty, loud environments. They run Android. They're not sleek but they're functional. Meta's Ray-Ban glasses have a camera but no display overlay. The gap is a glasses form factor that has *both* a good outward-facing camera *and* a see-through AR display, rugged enough for forest work. That sweet spot doesn't quite exist off the shelf yet, though it's close.
- **On-device ML inference.** Models like Segment Anything (SAM) can identify the boundary of a log in an image very fast, and smaller vision models run on a phone chip without internet.

**What's still genuinely hard:**

- **Rock-solid AR line stability on an irregular, moving surface** (because you're walking around and the log is organic, not a flat wall). Drift and jitter in the overlay is the main enemy here. Solvable with good SLAM (simultaneous localisation and mapping) but needs tuning.
- **Grain/knot-aware cut planning.** Reading the internal structure of a log to suggest the *best* cut pattern (not just a straight line) requires either an X-ray or a very good surface read. For MVP purposes, you'd ignore this and just do straight cuts.
- **An integrated hardware package.** Right now you'd be duct-taping a phone to existing safety glasses, which is fine for prototyping.

---

## 3. A Staged Experimental Plan

**Stage 0 — Right now, this week (zero cost):**
Grab a fallen log and your phone. Use an app like **Polycam** or **Scaniverse** (both free, LiDAR-based on iPhone) to scan the log and get a 3D mesh. This tells you immediately how good the geometry capture is. Then open **Reality Composer** (Apple, free) and try anchoring a simple line plane to that mesh. You'll learn fast whether the AR tracking holds as you move. This is the core experiment — does the line stay on the log?

**Stage 1 — Cheap cobbled prototype (under ~$200):**
Mount a phone in a pair of safety glasses using a 3D-printed or off-the-shelf phone mount. Use ARKit/ARCore with a simple app (or find an existing AR ruler/measurement app) to project a straight cut line onto the log surface. At this point you're not solving the "AI figures out the best cut pattern" part — you're just asking: *can I see a stable AR line on a log and use it as a guide while I move?* That's the revolutionary principle you want to test.

**Stage 2 — Tighten the feedback loop:**
If Stage 1 feels promising, write (or commission) a small iOS/Android app: point camera at log → tap to set cut plane → line appears and stays. Share it with your friends in the forest. Real sawing not required yet — even just walking around a log with the line projected is enough to know if it "feels" right.

**Stage 3 — Add the AI cut planning:**
Only once Stage 2 works well does it make sense to add computer vision that analyses the log dimensions and suggests a cut pattern. At this point you're integrating something like a vision API or a local model. This is months away, not days.

---

## 4. The MVP Core Principle

The revolutionary idea, stripped down: **replace rigid aluminium guide rails with a spatially-anchored AR line.** That's it. Everything else is downstream of whether that works. The cheapest possible test is a phone in a glasses mount, an AR app, and a log. No chainsaw required for the test. You could do a version of this *today* in your living room with a piece of wood on the floor.

This is the part I find most interesting to respond to.

A few honest observations:

The **"send to multiple LLMs and compare" workflow** is useful but creates a follow-up reading burden that you already noticed stresses you out. Your instinct to be **process-oriented** is right, but there's a specific trap here: generating ideas feels productive and *is* enjoyable, but the gap between idea and first physical experiment can quietly widen if you keep generating. One practical suggestion: at the end of a session like this, before you close the tab, write **one next physical action** with a time budget. Something like: *"Download Scaniverse, scan the woodpile in the garden, 15 minutes Saturday morning."* That's the bridge between ideation and reality.

On the **real work / bills tension** — this is a real constraint and it's worth being honest with yourself about it. The good news is that Stage 0 and Stage 1 of this project are genuinely weekend-level time investments. You don't need to quit your job to find out if the core idea has legs. Keep the early experiments tiny and cheap.

The safety glasses framing at the end of your note is one of the sharpest insights in the whole piece, by the way. "You'd be wearing them anyway" is a genuinely good product principle — you're not asking anyone to change their behaviour, you're just adding capability to something they already accept. That's worth keeping front and centre if you ever pitch this more formally.
