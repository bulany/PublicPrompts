# Response 1
What you're describing is genuinely sophisticated as a learning philosophy, and I think you've arrived at something that the deliberate practice research actually *supports* but rarely articulates well — so let me reflect back what I think you're getting at before diving in.

---

## What You're Actually Describing

You've intuitively separated two things that most "how to learn X" advice conflates: **acquisition** and **performance**. Most Rubik's cube tutorials optimize entirely for performance — here's the algorithm, memorize it, done. But they skip the entire acquisition phase, which is where real understanding (and real satisfaction) lives.

What you want is to *construct* an understanding of the cube rather than *receive* one. That's a meaningfully different cognitive experience, and it produces meaningfully different knowledge — the kind that's flexible, robust, and genuinely yours. The person who follows a tutorial can solve the cube; the person who figures it out can *think about* the cube.

The deliberate practice angle is interesting here because Ericsson's work is sometimes misread as "always structured, always goal-directed." But in his research on early skill development, particularly in musicians, there's a consistent finding: the best eventual performers almost always began with a period of **play** — unstructured, intrinsically motivated exploration — *before* structured practice kicked in. You're not avoiding deliberate practice; you're doing its necessary precursor.

---

## On the Time Budget

2–5 minutes a day is genuinely enough, and here's why it's arguably *better* than longer sessions for this particular goal:

The cube is partly a motor-memory problem (your hands need to learn move sequences automatically) and partly a conceptual problem (you need to build a mental model of how it behaves). Both of these consolidate during sleep. Short daily sessions mean you're constantly triggering consolidation cycles. A 30-minute session once a week is neurologically inferior for this kind of learning.

My honest estimate for your timeline — discovering your own method, not following one — is **3 to 5 months** to reliably solve it. That might sound long, but consider: the discovery itself *is* the experience you're after, and most of those months will feel like play, not work. You're not trying to win a race.

One structural suggestion: you don't need "rest days" in the traditional sense, because your sessions are short enough that fatigue isn't a factor. But **vary the intensity** — some days are pure play (5 minutes of messing around), some days you're working a specific puzzle you've set yourself. That variation keeps motivation high.

---

## The First Two Weeks: Games, Not Practice

Here are games roughly in the order I'd introduce them to yourself. Don't rush between them — stay in each one until it feels genuinely comfortable and a little boring.

**Game 1: Just watch** *(Days 1–3 or so)*
Scramble the cube. Don't try to solve anything. Just make moves and *watch what happens*. Follow a single piece — pick one corner, say the blue-red-white corner — and just watch where it goes as you turn faces. Notice that some moves bring it back; notice that it cycles. You're building intuition, not knowledge. There's nothing to achieve here.

**Game 2: The Undo Game** *(Days 3–7)*
Make one move. Undo it. Make two moves. Undo them in reverse. Work up to five or six moves. This sounds trivial, but it teaches you something profound about the cube's structure: it has memory, and that memory is perfectly reversible. Your hands start to learn what "undoing" feels like. This is laying groundwork you'll use later without knowing it yet.

**Game 3: Follow the Piece Home** *(Week 2)*
Pick one piece — start with an edge piece, they're simpler than corners. Scramble the cube lightly. Now try to get just that one piece back to where it belongs, using whatever moves feel natural. Don't worry about anything else on the cube. You'll almost certainly mess up other pieces doing it — that's fine, that's the game. What you're learning is that the cube resists local fixes: almost every move that helps one piece disturbs others. That *tension* is the central puzzle of the cube, and feeling it directly is more valuable than being told about it.

**Game 4: The Pretty Pattern Game** *(end of Week 2, into Week 3)*
Look up "Rubik's cube pretty patterns" — there are a handful of beautiful symmetric states (checkerboard, crosses, flowers) that can be reached with simple repeated moves. Try to make them. This isn't cheating or being given the solution — it's a completely different thing. What it does is show you that the cube has deep regularities, that certain move sequences produce beautiful, predictable results. It also makes your play sessions feel rewarding even when you're not "progressing."

---

## What Comes After (Without Spoiling It)

I'll be deliberately vague here, but I'll say this: the natural next games involve asking *"can I move this piece without disturbing pieces I've already placed?"* — and the attempt to answer that question is where people who discover the cube themselves tend to arrive at something like a genuine solution. You'll hit a wall, probably around weeks 4–6, where you can solve part of the cube but can't finish it without wrecking what you've done. That wall is not a sign of failure. It's the most interesting part of the whole experience. Sit with it. Play with it. It contains the solution.

---

## One Final Thing

You asked whether this makes sense to me, and what I think you're really getting at. I think you're running an experiment on yourself — testing whether the *process* of learning something can be as satisfying as the outcome, if you design the process right. The Rubik's cube is almost ideal for this because the solution exists, it's reachable, and there are natural intermediate milestones. You're not wandering in the dark forever.

And yes, I think it's completely feasible. The people who enjoy solving cubes most aren't the ones who memorized algorithms in an afternoon. They're the ones who spent time actually *playing* with the thing.
