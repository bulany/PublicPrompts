# Response 1



What you are proposing is a fascinating, beautiful experiment in **first-principles thinking, constructivist learning, and micro-dosing cognitive strain**. 

What you are *actually* getting at here is the desire to experience the pure "Eureka" of discovery rather than the mechanical satisfaction of rote memorization. Most people learn the Rubik's Cube by memorizing algorithms (essentially running someone else's software on their brain). You are trying to write the software from scratch. You want to see if the subconscious mind—given highly structured, bite-sized inputs—can organically build an intuition for 3D spatial group theory. 

Here is my feedback on your proposal, a realistic timeline, a time-budget strategy based on deliberate practice, and the starter "games" to get you going.

### The Reality Check & Timeline Estimate
Can you do this? **Yes.** Ernő Rubik, the inventor of the cube, famously took about a month to solve it himself after creating it, and he didn't have algorithms to look up either. 

However, Rubik was spending hours a day on it. With a time budget of 2 to 5 minutes a day (roughly 15–35 minutes a week), you are relying heavily on the **spacing effect**—the phenomenon where your brain continues to process and consolidate complex spatial problems in the background (diffuse mode) while you sleep and go about your day. 

**Estimate:** If you stick to this purely by self-discovery, it will likely take you anywhere from **4 to 8 months** to robustly solve the cube. But because you are spacing it out, your *actual time spent* will be remarkably low (maybe 10 to 20 hours total).

### The Time Budget Strategy (Deliberate Practice)
Deliberate practice requires intense focus, operating at the edge of your ability, and immediate feedback. Two to five minutes is actually perfect for intense focus, but it is a very short time to set up a problem and solve it. 

Instead of a strict 3 minutes every single day, I recommend a **Hybrid Batching Strategy**:
*   **The "Deep Play" Days (2 days a week - e.g., Tuesday, Saturday):** Spend **10 minutes** on these days. This gives you enough runway to try a complex idea, fail, and try again without the timer running out immediately. 
*   **The "Reinforcement" Days (3 days a week):** Spend **2 to 3 minutes**. On these days, you do not try to discover anything new. You just play the games you figured out on your Deep Play days to solidify your muscle memory and spatial intuition.
*   **The "Incubation" Days (2 days a week):** **0 minutes.** Do not touch the cube. Let your subconscious mind chew on the spatial relationships. 

### The Starter Games (Weeks 1–4)
To discover the solution, you cannot just randomly twist the cube—that is just playing, not deliberate practice. You need to isolate variables. Here are a few "games" to play during your sessions that will naturally build the exact intuition you need to invent your own solution.

**Game 1: The Detective (Tracking)**
*   **How to play:** Focus on *one specific piece* (e.g., the White-Red-Blue corner). Scramble the cube with exactly 3 random twists. Now, try to get that *one piece* back to where it belongs, facing the correct way.
*   **Level up:** Increase to 4 twists, then 5. 
*   **The Goal:** You are learning how pieces move through 3D space. You’ll start to notice that corners always stay corners, and edges always stay edges. 

**Game 2: The Commuter (Orientation)**
*   **How to play:** Pick an edge piece (a piece with two colors). Move it around the cube on a little "journey" of 5 or 6 moves, and then bring it back to its exact starting position. 
*   **The catch:** When it arrives back home, is it flipped upside down? Or is it facing the same way it started? 
*   **The Goal:** Try to figure out *what types of journeys* flip the piece, and *what types of journeys* keep it the same. This is a massive secret to solving the cube later.

**Game 3: Hide and Seek (Preservation)**
*   **How to play:** Solve just a tiny part of the cube (like a 2x2 block in one corner, or just a line of 3 white pieces). Now, your goal is to move a piece on the *opposite* side of the cube, without permanently destroying the block you built.
*   **The technique to discover:** You will naturally have to figure out how to "hide" your built block out of the way, make your move on the rest of the cube, and "unhide" the block. 
*   **The Goal:** This is the foundation of commutators—the mathematical concept that allows you to move specific pieces without destroying the rest of the solved cube. 

**Game 4: The Reverser (Working Backwards)**
*   **How to play:** Close your eyes, do exactly 4 random moves. Open your eyes. Figure out how to undo them to get back to the solved state (you'll need to start with a solved cube for this, which you might have to ask a friend to do for you initially, or use an app just to reset it to a solved state to practice this specific game).
*   **The Goal:** This teaches your brain sequence memory and inverse operations.

### How to transition from "Playing" to "Solving"
After a few weeks of these games, your brain will stop seeing a chaotic jumble of 54 stickers. You will start seeing the cube as a skeleton of a fixed center, with 8 corners and 12 edges moving along very specific tracks. 

Once you reach that point, your game changes. You stop playing the mini-games and you start the ultimate game: **"How much of the cube can I build before my moves start destroying what I've already built?"** 

You will likely find it easy to build one side. Then, you will get stuck. At that point, your play sessions will revolve purely around finding a specific sequence of moves that allows you to swap three pieces without breaking the bottom of the cube. 

Enjoy the process. It will be incredibly frustrating at times, but if you treat it as a sandbox rather than a test, the moment you invent your own method to swap those final pieces will be profoundly rewarding.
